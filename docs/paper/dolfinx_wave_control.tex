\title{\bfseries In-Plane Orthotropic Elastic Wave Control by
Fiber-Orientation Design}
\author{}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We give a self-contained \texttt{FEniCSx}/\texttt{dolfinx} finite-element
formulation for steering in-plane (vector) elastic waves in a continuous-fiber
composite by designing \emph{only} the local fiber orientation $\theta(\vx)$,
together with the full discrete-adjoint derivation of the design sensitivity.
The physics is the genuine two-dimensional orthotropic elastodynamics used in the
reference implementation --- rotated fiber plane-stress stiffness
$\mathbf D_f(\theta)=\mathbf T(\theta)\,\mathbf Q_f\,\mathbf T(\theta)\T$ with a
strong modulus contrast ($E_{f1}/E_{f2}\approx15$) --- \emph{not} a reduced
scalar antiplane model. Because $\mathbf D_f(\theta)$ is independent of $\theta$
exactly when the material is isotropic, fiber orientation is a dead design
variable for an isotropic medium: the fiber orthotropy is the necessary and
sufficient enabling mechanism for orientation control. The complex time-harmonic
problem is solved in a real \texttt{PETSc} build via an exact real four-field
split, the orientation is regularized by a smooth CS-RBF support-point map for
manufacturable toolpaths, and every gradient is obtained by automatic UFL
differentiation of the residual (finite-difference verified to $\sim\!10^{-6}$).
The resulting pipeline reproduces the two canonical demonstrations of the
reference work: a curvilinear-fiber \emph{wave lens} that focuses an incident
plane wave with a symmetric $78\times$ focus-energy gain, and an \emph{elastic cloak} that
wraps its fibers around a conforming traction-free void and restores the far field with a $36\times$
scattering reduction. We then extend the same constitutive model to \emph{periodic}
media, co-designing topology and toolpath on a Bloch cell so a chosen band is
flattened and isolated by complete gaps on both sides, and validating the design by
re-simulating the \emph{as-manufactured} cell --- cropped topology plus delineated,
exactly cell-periodic fiber tows. The complete algorithmic specification is given:
the operator inventory, both composed gradient chains, pseudocode for both
pipelines, and a data-flow diagram. We are explicit about scope: the wave-control
results are computed in \texttt{dolfinx}, while the band-structure results come
from an independent \texttt{numpy}/\texttt{scipy} Q4 code that shares the physics
and the design machinery but none of the assembly or linear algebra.
\end{abstract}

\section{Contributions and relation to prior work}
\label{sec:contrib}
The manufacturing-side machinery we use --- a CS-RBF continuous fiber-orientation
field, the wave-projection scheme that renders that field as finite-pitch
toolpaths, and a \emph{local fiber-curvature constraint} tied to a prescribed
minimum turning radius --- is that of Wong, Sanders and
Rosen~\cite{wong2025} (who in turn adopt the wave projection of Ren et al.).
That body of work designs \emph{static} structures: compliance minimization under
a volume constraint, with the toolpath and its curvature limit integrated into
each optimization iteration. We do \emph{not} claim those ingredients as new.

Our contribution is to carry that framework into \emph{time-harmonic elastic wave
control} and to give it a complete, verifiable \texttt{FEniCSx} realization. The
specific new components, relative to \cite{wong2025} and to our own earlier
\texttt{numpy} prototype, are:
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{Wave-control objectives instead of compliance.} The design steers a
propagating in-plane elastic wave: a \emph{lens} that maximizes focus energy
$\int_{\rm reg}|\vu|^2$ and a \emph{cloak} that minimizes the far-field mismatch
$\int_{\rm obs}|\vu-\vu_{\rm ref}|^2$ to a void-free reference
(\S\ref{sec:adj}) --- a different physical regime (elastodynamics, complex-valued
fields) from static stiffness design.
\item \textbf{Exact real four-field split} of the complex time-harmonic vector
problem \eqref{eq:complex}--\eqref{eq:block}, so a \emph{real} \texttt{PETSc}
build solves it with the correct structural damping and absorbing sponge.
\item \textbf{Automatic discrete adjoint} for these objectives by UFL
differentiation of the residual \eqref{eq:adj}--\eqref{eq:sens} (two lines of
code, finite-difference verified to $\sim\!10^{-6}$), rather than a hand-coded
compliance sensitivity.
\item \textbf{An exact isotropy-annihilation result} (Remark~\ref{rem:iso}):
fiber orientation is a provably \emph{dead} design variable for an isotropic
medium ($\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial\theta\equiv0$), so the whole sensitivity
vanishes; anisotropy (fiber contrast $E_{f1}/E_{f2}$) is the \emph{necessary and
sufficient} enabling mechanism for orientation-based wave control. This gives the
method a clean baseline: the identical code returns exactly the isotropic response
at zero design authority.
\item \textbf{Curvature limit identified with a prescribed circular through-hole}
($\zeta_{\rm all}=1/R$), applied to \emph{both} wave-control problems
(\S\ref{sec:hole}), including the honest finding that a cloak cannot meet the exact
$1/R_V$ bound without losing its function.
\item \textbf{Phase-field toolpaths and anisotropy director maps for the
wave-control designs} (\S\ref{sec:rbf}, \S\ref{sec:orient}), with a
least-squares gradient-matching \eqref{eq:phase} that keeps a constant tow pitch
(the naive normal-equations phase does not).
\item \textbf{Co-designed flat-band / band-gap metamaterials} (\S\ref{sec:meta}):
the same orthotropic model on a periodic Bloch cell, with \emph{topology} (density)
and \emph{toolpath} (orientation) optimized \emph{together} --- via eigenvalue
sensitivities and KS aggregation --- to flatten a chosen band and isolate it by a
gap, neither design field alone sufficing.
\item \textbf{An exactly cell-periodic toolpath construction}
(Appendix~\ref{app:perphase}): on a periodic cell the phase field cannot be pinned,
so we split it into a Fourier-space Helmholtz projection \eqref{eq:fftphase} plus
an \emph{integer} number of tow cycles per period \eqref{eq:towcount}. This makes
the tows join across every cell boundary exactly, at a quantified pitch-distortion
cost \eqref{eq:pitcherr} that decays with the pitch --- and it lets us re-simulate
the \emph{as-manufactured} cell rather than only the SIMP design.
\item \textbf{A complete algorithmic specification} (\S\ref{sec:algo}): the full
operator inventory, the two composed gradient chains
\eqref{eq:chainA}--\eqref{eq:chainBfull}, pseudocode for both pipelines
(Algorithms~\ref{alg:wave}--\ref{alg:flat}) and a data-flow diagram
(Figure~\ref{fig:flow}), so every step is reproducible from the text alone.
\end{enumerate}
Everything is traceable to the code: each equation names its implementing function
(Appendix~\ref{app:codemap}) and every constant is tabulated
(Appendix~\ref{app:params}).

\paragraph{Scope of each solver.} Two independent codes produce these results and
we keep the distinction explicit throughout: the wave-control results
(\S\ref{sec:results}--\S\ref{sec:hole}) are computed in
\texttt{dolfinx}/\texttt{FEniCSx} with \texttt{PETSc} and UFL-generated adjoints,
whereas the periodic band-structure results (\S\ref{sec:meta}) come from a separate,
self-contained \texttt{numpy}/\texttt{scipy} Q4 implementation that uses no
\texttt{dolfinx} at all (Remark~\ref{rem:solvers}). They share the constitutive
model and the design machinery, not the assembly or the linear algebra.

\section{Governing model and the anisotropy lever}
\label{sec:model}
Consider in-plane time-harmonic motion $\vu(\vx)e^{-\ii\omega t}$,
$\vu=(u_x,u_y)$, of a thin composite plate $\Omega\subset\mathbb R^2$ under
plane-stress. With the engineering Voigt strain
\begin{equation}
  \veps(\vu)=\bigl[\,\partial_x u_x,\ \ \partial_y u_y,\ \
  \partial_y u_x+\partial_x u_y\,\bigr]\T,
\end{equation}
the fiber composite has the rotated orthotropic plane-stress stiffness
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf D_f(\theta)=\mathbf T(\theta)\,\mathbf Q_f\,\mathbf T(\theta)\T,\qquad
  \mathbf T(\theta)=\begin{bmatrix} c^2 & s^2 & -2sc\\ s^2 & c^2 & 2sc\\
  sc & -sc & c^2-s^2\end{bmatrix},\ \ (c,s)=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta),
  \label{eq:Df}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf Q_f$ is the fiber stiffness in material axes,
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf Q_f=\begin{bmatrix} Q_{11}&Q_{12}&0\\ Q_{12}&Q_{22}&0\\
  0&0&Q_{66}\end{bmatrix},\quad
  Q_{11}=\tfrac{E_{f1}}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\ \
  Q_{22}=\tfrac{E_{f2}}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\ \
  Q_{12}=\tfrac{\nu_{12}E_{f2}}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\ \ Q_{66}=G_{12},
\end{equation}
with $\nu_{21}=\nu_{12}E_{f2}/E_{f1}$. We use the reference material
$E_{f1}=131,\ E_{f2}=9,\ G_{12}=5,\ \nu_{12}=0.27$ (a modulus contrast
$E_{f1}/E_{f2}\approx14.6$), density $\rho=1.6$. The time-harmonic displacement
obeys
\begin{equation}
  -\nabla\!\cdot\!\bigl[(1+\ii\eta)\,\bm\sigma(\vu;\theta)\bigr]
  -\omega^2\rho\,\vu-\ii\,\omega\,c\,\vu=\mathbf f,\qquad
  \bm\sigma=\mathbf D_f(\theta)\,\veps(\vu),
  \label{eq:strong}
\end{equation}
where $\eta$ is a small structural (hysteretic) damping that keeps the response
off sharp undamped resonances (we use $\eta=0.02$--$0.03$; without it the field is
speckle), and $c(\vx)\ge0$ is a mass-proportional absorbing sponge
(\S\ref{sec:sponge}). The orientation $\theta(\vx)$ is the sole design field.

\begin{remark}[Isotropy annihilates the orientation lever]
\label{rem:iso}
The stiffness of an \emph{isotropic} material is a rotationally invariant tensor:
if $\mathbf Q_f$ is isotropic ($Q_{11}=Q_{22}$ and $Q_{66}=\tfrac12(Q_{11}-Q_{12})$)
then $\mathbf T(\theta)\mathbf Q_f\mathbf T(\theta)\T=\mathbf Q_f$ for every
$\theta$, so $\mathbf D_f(\theta)$ --- and hence the entire operator
\eqref{eq:strong} --- is \emph{independent of $\theta$}. Consequently
$\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial\theta\equiv\mathbf 0$, every orientation
sensitivity below (which we show is built from $\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial
\theta$) vanishes identically, and fiber orientation is a \emph{dead design
variable} for an isotropic plate. The fiber orthotropy ($E_{f1}\neq E_{f2}$,
$G_{12}\neq\tfrac12(Q_{11}-Q_{12})$) is therefore the necessary and sufficient
condition for orientation-based wave control, and its authority grows with the
modulus contrast $E_{f1}/E_{f2}$. The strong contrast of continuous carbon fiber
is exactly what makes the lens and cloak below possible.
\end{remark}

\section{Finite-element discretization in \texttt{dolfinx}}
\label{sec:fem}

\subsection{Function spaces, element types, and the mesh for each problem}
\label{sec:spaces}
Because two solvers and three mesh types appear in this work, we state the
discrete spaces explicitly before deriving anything. Table~\ref{tab:spaces}
collects them.

The state space for the wave-control problems is a \emph{four-component}
continuous Lagrange space,
\begin{equation}
  \vU_h\in\mathcal W_h=\bigl[\mathbb P_1(\mathcal T_h)\bigr]^4
  =\operatorname{span}\{\text{\texttt{("Lagrange",1,(4,))}}\},
  \qquad
  \vU=(u_x^r,u_y^r,u_x^i,u_y^i),
  \label{eq:Wspace}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ \emph{four} scalar degrees of freedom per vertex: the two in-plane
components of the real part and the two of the imaginary part. This is the
discrete carrier of the real four-field split \eqref{eq:block} --- it is why a
real \texttt{PETSc} build suffices, and it is the reason the block operator is
$4\times4$ rather than $2\times2$.

The design fields live in the \emph{discontinuous, element-wise constant} space
\begin{equation}
  \theta_h,\ z_h\in\mathcal S_h=\mathbb{DG}_0(\mathcal T_h)
  =\operatorname{span}\{\text{\texttt{("DG",0)}}\},
\end{equation}
one orientation (and one soft-void factor) per cell. The pairing
$\mathcal W_h=[\mathbb P_1]^4$ with $\mathcal S_h=\mathbb{DG}_0$ is deliberate:
the state must be conforming in $H^1$, whereas the design must be free to vary
cell-by-cell without any inter-element continuity, and the cell gradient
\eqref{eq:sens} is then obtained simply by differentiating the residual against
the $\mathbb{DG}_0$ test function.

\paragraph{The adjoint is solved in the same space.} We use a Galerkin
discretization, so the adjoint state
$\bm\Lambda_h\in\mathcal W_h$ --- the \emph{same} $[\mathbb P_1]^4$ space as the
forward state, on the same mesh. Discretely the adjoint operator is the transpose
$\mathcal A_h\T$ of the forward matrix, not a separately discretized continuous
adjoint; this ``discretize-then-differentiate'' order is what makes the gradient
exact for the discrete objective (and is why it matches finite differences to
$\sim\!10^{-6}$ rather than to truncation error). Practically, the forward solve
factors $\mathcal A_h=\mathbf{LU}$ and the adjoint reuses those factors with a
transposed triangular solve, so the adjoint is nearly free.

\paragraph{Meshes.} The lens and the multi-target problems use a structured
right-triangle mesh of $[0,L_x]\times[0,L_y]$
(\texttt{dolfinx.mesh.create\_rectangle}). The through-hole problems instead use a
\texttt{gmsh}/OCC mesh in which each circular hole is \emph{cut from the geometry}
so the triangulation \emph{conforms} to the circle, refined on the rim
(\texttt{wavetopo/dolfinx\_mesh.py:rect\_mesh}); the hole boundary is then exactly
circular and traction-free by the natural boundary condition of the weak form. We
flag the distinction because it is the substantive reason for using
\texttt{dolfinx} here at all: representing a hole instead as a soft
$\mathbb{DG}_0$ density void on a structured grid staircases the boundary to
within one element, scatters off the staircase corners, and blurs the very radius
$R$ that the curvature rule $\zeta_{\rm all}=1/R$ is tied to.

\paragraph{The periodic cell.} The metamaterial of \S\ref{sec:meta} is discretized
differently again (Remark~\ref{rem:solvers}): structured \emph{bilinear
quadrilateral} (Q4) elements, two displacement degrees of freedom per node
(8 per element), with $2\times2$ Gauss quadrature for both the stiffness and the
\emph{consistent} mass matrix, and element-wise constant density and orientation.
A structured quadrilateral grid is the natural choice there --- the Bloch
reduction $\mathbf T(\mathbf k)$ becomes index arithmetic, one element matrix is
reused for every cell, and the design lattice supports the periodic filter
\eqref{eq:filter} and the FFT phase \eqref{eq:fftphase} directly. Conforming
meshing would buy nothing on that problem, because there the geometry \emph{is}
the density field: there is no prescribed boundary to conform to.

\subsection{Real four-field split of the complex vector problem}
The weak form of \eqref{eq:strong} is the complex-symmetric system
\begin{equation}
  \bigl[(1+\ii\eta)\mathbf K(\vth)-\omega^2\mathbf M+\ii\omega\mathbf C\bigr]\vu
  =\mathbf f,\quad
  \mathbf K=\!\int_\Omega\!\veps(\cdot)\T\mathbf D_f(\theta)\veps(\cdot),\ \
  \mathbf M=\!\int_\Omega\!\rho\,(\cdot)\!\cdot\!(\cdot),\ \
  \mathbf C=\!\int_\Omega\!c\,(\cdot)\!\cdot\!(\cdot).
  \label{eq:complex}
\end{equation}
The \texttt{PETSc} build stores \emph{real} scalars only, so we split
$\vu=\vu_r+\ii\vu_i$ (each a 2-vector) and solve the equivalent real block system
on the four-component field $U=(u_{xr},u_{yr},u_{xi},u_{yi})$,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{bmatrix} A & -B\\ B & \phantom{-}A\end{bmatrix}
  \begin{bmatrix} \vu_r\\ \vu_i\end{bmatrix}
  =\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf f_r\\ \mathbf f_i\end{bmatrix},\qquad
  A=\mathbf K(\vth)-\omega^2\mathbf M,\qquad B=\eta\,\mathbf K(\vth)+\omega\mathbf C,
  \label{eq:block}
\end{equation}
which is exactly $[\Real,-\Imag;\Imag,\Real]$ of the complex operator (the
structural damping $\eta\mathbf K$ joins the sponge in the imaginary block $B$).

\paragraph{Where \eqref{eq:block} comes from.} Write the complex operator as
$\mathcal A_{\mathbb C}=A+\ii B$ with $A=\mathbf K-\omega^2\mathbf M$ and
$B=\eta\mathbf K+\omega\mathbf C$ \emph{both real and symmetric}. Expanding the
complex product and separating real and imaginary parts,
\begin{equation}
  (A+\ii B)(\vu_r+\ii\vu_i)
  =\underbrace{\bigl(A\vu_r-B\vu_i\bigr)}_{\text{real part}}
  +\ \ii\underbrace{\bigl(B\vu_r+A\vu_i\bigr)}_{\text{imaginary part}}
  \ \overset{!}{=}\ \mathbf f_r+\ii\mathbf f_i ,
\end{equation}
and equating the two parts \emph{separately} --- legitimate because
$\{1,\ii\}$ is a basis of $\mathbb C$ over $\mathbb R$ --- gives the two coupled
real equations $A\vu_r-B\vu_i=\mathbf f_r$ and $B\vu_r+A\vu_i=\mathbf f_i$, i.e.\
exactly \eqref{eq:block}. This is the standard \emph{realification}, the injective
ring homomorphism
$a+\ii b\ \mapsto\ \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}a&-b\\ b&\phantom{-}a\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$
applied blockwise; nothing is approximated, dropped, or symmetrized.

\begin{proposition}[Exactness of the split and of its adjoint]
\label{prop:split}
Let $A,B\in\mathbb R^{n\times n}$ be symmetric and
$\mathcal A=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}A&-B\\ B&\phantom{-}A\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.
Then
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0pt
\item $(\vu_r,\vu_i)$ solves $\mathcal A(\vu_r,\vu_i)\T=(\mathbf f_r,\mathbf f_i)\T$
if and only if $\vu=\vu_r+\ii\vu_i$ solves $(A+\ii B)\vu=\mathbf f_r+\ii\mathbf f_i$;
in particular $\mathcal A$ is invertible exactly when $A+\ii B$ is.
\item $\mathcal A\T=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}A&B\\ -B&A\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$
is the realification of $A-\ii B=(A+\ii B)\HH$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(1) is the displayed computation read in both directions. For (2), transposing
blockwise and using $A\T=A$, $B\T=B$ gives
$\mathcal A\T=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}A&B\\ -B&A\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$, which
is the image of $A-\ii B$ under
$a+\ii b\mapsto\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}a&-b\\ b&a\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$; and
$(A+\ii B)\HH=A\HH-\ii B\HH=A-\ii B$ since $A,B$ are real symmetric.
\end{proof}

Part (2) is the reason the real formulation costs us nothing in the optimization:
the discrete adjoint equation \eqref{eq:adj} is solved with the \emph{transpose}
$\mathcal A\T$, and Proposition~\ref{prop:split} says that transposing the real
block system is precisely applying the complex \emph{Hermitian} adjoint. So the
real solve returns the true complex adjoint state, not an approximation of it ---
which is why the resulting gradient matches finite differences to
$\sim\!10^{-6}$.

\begin{remark}[Cost, and when the split can be dropped]
\label{rem:cost}
$\mathcal A$ is real but \emph{non}-symmetric whenever $B\neq0$ (i.e.\ whenever
there is damping or a sponge), so it is factored by sparse LU, not Cholesky. The
split doubles the unknowns, and a real LU on $2n$ unknowns costs
$\tfrac23(2n)^3=8\cdot\tfrac23n^3$ against $\approx4\cdot\tfrac23n^3$ for a native
complex LU on $n$ complex unknowns (a complex multiply being four real
multiplies): the realification is roughly a \emph{factor of two} more arithmetic
than a genuine complex build. We pay it because the \texttt{PETSc} installed here
is a real (\texttt{float64}) build --- the environment name notwithstanding ---
and the split is exact. With a complex-scalar \texttt{PETSc}/\texttt{SLEPc} the
entire construction of this subsection collapses to assembling
\eqref{eq:complex} directly on a two-component complex space, and only the
objective (which is already real-valued) needs care. Nothing else in the paper
would change: the adjoint, the sensitivity \eqref{eq:sens} and every reported
number are properties of the discrete problem, not of how its scalars are stored.
\end{remark}

We discretize $U$ on the four-component continuous $P_1$ space
\eqref{eq:Wspace} and assemble \eqref{eq:block} as a single bilinear form. Writing the stiffness pairing
$s(\mathbf a,\mathbf b)=\int_\Omega\veps(\mathbf a)\T\mathbf D_f(\theta)\veps(\mathbf b)$
and the mass/sponge pairings $m(\mathbf a,\mathbf b)=\int_\Omega\rho\,\mathbf a
\!\cdot\!\mathbf b$, $d(\mathbf a,\mathbf b)=\int_\Omega c\,\mathbf a\!\cdot\!
\mathbf b$ for the test 2-vectors $\mathbf p_r,\mathbf p_i$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  a(U,P;\vth)=\ &s(\vu_r,\mathbf p_r)-\omega^2 m(\vu_r,\mathbf p_r)
  -\eta\,s(\vu_i,\mathbf p_r)-\omega\,d(\vu_i,\mathbf p_r)\\[-1pt]
  +\ &s(\vu_i,\mathbf p_i)-\omega^2 m(\vu_i,\mathbf p_i)
  +\eta\,s(\vu_r,\mathbf p_i)+\omega\,d(\vu_r,\mathbf p_i),\qquad
  \ell(P)=\int_\Omega\mathbf f\!\cdot\!\mathbf p_r .
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:aform}
\end{equation}
The orientation $\theta$, density $\hat z$, sponge $c$ and region indicators are
element-wise ($DG_0$) fields; $\mathbf D_f(\theta)$ is built from \eqref{eq:Df}
directly in UFL. A single-material two-phase SIMP density enters as
$\mathbf D_f\!\mapsto\!(z_\varepsilon+(1-z_\varepsilon)\hat z^{\,p})\mathbf D_f$
and $\rho\!\mapsto\!\hat z\rho_f+(1-\hat z)\rho_m$ ($p=3$, $z_\varepsilon=10^{-4}$)
to carve the soft void of the cloak; for the lens $\hat z\equiv1$. We assemble
\eqref{eq:aform} and solve with a sparse LU factorization; write the discrete
system $\mathcal A(\vth)\,\vU=\vb$.

\subsection{Absorbing sponge and drive}
\label{sec:sponge}
The sponge coefficient ramps quadratically into a boundary margin of width $m$,
\begin{equation}
  c(\vx)=c_0\Bigl[\max\Bigl(0,\ \tfrac{x-(L_x-m)}{m},\ \tfrac{m-y}{m},\
  \tfrac{y-(L_y-m)}{m}\Bigr)\Bigr]^2 ,
  \label{eq:sponge}
\end{equation}
opening the right, top and bottom edges (the left edge carries the drive), so the
finite plate radiates as if unbounded. The drive is an $x$-directed line source
concentrated near the left edge,
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf f(\vx)=\bigl(A\,e^{-\beta (x-x_0)^2},\,0\bigr),\qquad
  (A,\beta,x_0)=(30,\,80,\,0.1),
  \label{eq:source}
\end{equation}
a plane pressure wave. Parameters $(c_0,m)$ are $(80,0.6)$ for the lens and
$(70,0.55)$ for the cloak (Table~\ref{tab:params}). Since $\mathbf C,\mathbf M$
and $\mathbf f$ are $\theta$-independent, they drop out of every design
sensitivity (\S\ref{sec:adj}); they are set by \texttt{set\_sponge},
\texttt{set\_source} in \texttt{wavetopo/dolfinx\_elastic.py}.

\section{Objectives and the discrete-adjoint sensitivity}
\label{sec:adj}
Both objectives are quadratic in the state, $J=\int_\Omega w\,\Psi(U)$ with $w$ a
region indicator:
\begin{equation}
  \Psi_{\rm lens}=|\vu|^2=\!\sum_{k=0}^{3}U_k^2,\qquad
  \Psi_{\rm cloak}=|\vu-\vu_{\rm ref}|^2=\!\sum_{k=0}^{3}(U_k-U_k^{\rm ref})^2,
\end{equation}
the focus energy in a target box (maximized) and the far-field mismatch to a
void-free reference (minimized).

\subsection{Lagrangian, adjoint equation, and residual derivative}
Adjoin the forward problem $\mathcal A(\vth)\vU-\vb=\mathbf0$ with a multiplier
$\bm\Lambda$: $\mathcal L=J(\vU)+\bm\Lambda\T(\mathcal A\vU-\vb)$. On the
constraint manifold $\mathcal L=J$, and expanding the total derivative,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\rmd J}{\rmd\theta_\ell}
  =\underbrace{\Bigl(\tfrac{\partial J}{\partial\vU}
  +\mathcal A\T\bm\Lambda\Bigr)\T\tfrac{\rmd\vU}{\rmd\theta_\ell}}
  _{\text{eliminated by the adjoint}}
  +\ \bm\Lambda\T\frac{\partial\mathcal A}{\partial\theta_\ell}\vU .
\end{equation}
Choosing $\bm\Lambda$ to kill the implicit state term gives the \emph{discrete
adjoint equation}, and the design derivative then reduces to the explicit
$\theta$-dependence of the residual:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\ \mathcal A(\vth)\T\bm\Lambda=-\frac{\partial J}{\partial\vU}\ }
  \qquad
  \boxed{\ \frac{\rmd J}{\rmd\theta_\ell}=\frac{\partial R}{\partial\theta_\ell},
  \quad R(\vth)\equiv a(\vU,\bm\Lambda;\vth)-\ell(\bm\Lambda)\ }
  \label{eq:adj}
\end{equation}
with $\vU,\bm\Lambda$ frozen. Only the stiffness terms in $R$ carry $\theta$, so
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\rmd J}{\rmd\theta_\ell}
  =\int_{\Omega_\ell}\!\Bigl[\veps(\vu_r)\T
  \tfrac{\partial\mathbf D_f}{\partial\theta}\veps(\bm\lambda_r)
  +\veps(\vu_i)\T\tfrac{\partial\mathbf D_f}{\partial\theta}\veps(\bm\lambda_i)
  +\eta\bigl(\veps(\vu_r)\T\tfrac{\partial\mathbf D_f}{\partial\theta}
  \veps(\bm\lambda_i)-\veps(\vu_i)\T\tfrac{\partial\mathbf D_f}{\partial\theta}
  \veps(\bm\lambda_r)\bigr)\Bigr],
  \label{eq:sens}
\end{equation}
where $\bm\lambda_r,\bm\lambda_i$ are the real/imag adjoint displacements and
$\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial\theta=\mathbf T'\mathbf Q_f\mathbf T\T+\mathbf T
\mathbf Q_f\mathbf T'^{\,\T}$. The mass, sponge and source carry no
$\theta$-dependence and drop out: the whole gradient is built from
$\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial\theta$, so it vanishes identically for an isotropic
material, making Remark~\ref{rem:iso} an exact statement about the gradient.

\subsection{Automatic implementation, scaling, and verification}
Each boxed step is one line of UFL. The adjoint load $\partial J/\partial\vU$ is
$\texttt{derivative}(w\Psi(U)\rmd\vx,U,P)$; \eqref{eq:adj} is solved with the
transposed operator $\mathcal A\T$ (reusing the LU factors); the cell gradient is
$\texttt{derivative}(R,\theta,\hat P)$ against the $DG_0$ test function --- no
element derivatives by hand. Both the lens and cloak gradients match central
finite differences to a relative error $\sim\!10^{-6}$. The raw objectives are
tiny ($\sim\!10^{-4}$), so for the lens we descend the scale-invariant
\emph{log}-objective $g_\ell=(\partial_{\theta_\ell}J)/J$, restoring $O(1)$
gradients for the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) under
$\theta_\ell\in[-\tfrac\pi2,\tfrac\pi2]$.

\subsection{Smooth orientation parametrization and manufacturable toolpaths}
\label{sec:rbf}
A raw per-cell orientation is mesh-scale noisy --- un-manufacturable and, driving
the wave field, incoherent. We therefore parametrize $\theta$ by a coarse grid of
support-point angles $\hat\theta=\{\hat\theta_i\}$ at sites $\{\mathbf s_i\}$
(spacing $h_s$) interpolated to the cell centroids $\{\vx_c\}$ with a compactly
supported Wendland $C^2$ radial basis $\phi(q)=(1-q)^4(4q+1)$ (support $q<1$):
\begin{equation}
  \theta_c=(B\hat\theta)_c=\sum_i B_{ci}\hat\theta_i,\qquad
  B_{ci}=\frac{\phi(q_{ci})}{\sum_j\phi(q_{cj})},\qquad
  q_{ci}=\frac{\lVert\vx_c-\mathbf s_i\rVert}{r},
  \label{eq:rbf}
\end{equation}
a row-stochastic map ($\sum_i B_{ci}=1$); $B\T$ propagates to the gradient,
$\partial J/\partial\hat\theta=B\T(\partial J/\partial\theta)$. Support spacing and
radius are $(h_s,r)=(0.33,0.7)$ for the lens and $(0.28,0.6)$ for the cloak
(\texttt{support\_map} in \texttt{wavetopo/dolfinx\_wave.py}). This CS-RBF map makes
the design low-dimensional and smooth \emph{by construction}. When the problem has a mirror symmetry (as the
lens does about $y=L_y/2$) we enforce it on the design: a fiber field is
symmetric under $y\!\mapsto\!L_y-y$ iff $\theta(x,L_y-y)=-\theta(x,y)$, so we use
a support grid symmetric about the axis and project the design each iteration
onto that subspace, $\hat\theta\mapsto\tfrac12(\hat\theta-\hat\theta_{\rm mirror})$.
This removes the spurious asymmetry that the (non-symmetric) triangulation and
optimizer would otherwise introduce, yielding a symmetric lens with a horizontal
fiber on the axis. We delineate the resulting fiber \emph{toolpaths} with the
reference-paper wave projection: from the sign-free double-angle director
$(\cos2\theta,\sin2\theta)$ we recover a smooth (seam-free) orientation branch by
region-growing sign propagation, then solve for a phase field $\psi$ whose gradient
is unit and orthogonal to the fibers,
\begin{equation}
  \nabla\psi(\vx)=\mathbf g\equiv[-\sin\theta,\ \cos\theta]\T,\qquad
  \psi=\arg\min_{\psi}\int_\Omega\lVert\nabla\psi-\mathbf g\rVert^2 ,
  \label{eq:phase}
\end{equation}
discretized as a sparse least-squares gradient match on the grid (\texttt{lsqr};
matching $\nabla\psi$ \emph{directly} keeps $|\nabla\psi|\!\approx\!1$, i.e.\ a
constant tow pitch --- the normal-equations Poisson form drops the boundary flux
and collapses the pitch). The evenly spaced level sets of $\psi$ --- equivalently
the peaks of the wave projection $\chi=\tfrac12+\tfrac12\cos(2\pi\psi/d)$ --- are
the as-manufactured tows at pitch $d$; they follow the fiber direction, keep a
fixed spacing, and are masked out of any prescribed void, so no toolpath crosses a
hole (\texttt{fiber\_phase}, \texttt{plot\_toolpaths\_phase} in
\texttt{wavetopo/dolfinx\_viz.py}). Together with the structural damping $\eta$,
this smoothing is what renders the optimized fields coherent rather than speckled.

\subsection{Fiber-curvature (curl) constraint}
\label{sec:curl}
A printable toolpath cannot bend arbitrarily tightly: the tow has a minimum
turning radius. Following the reference work, we bound the local
\emph{fiber-path curvature} --- the directional derivative of the orientation
along the fiber, $\vn=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$,
\begin{equation}
  \zeta(\vx)=(\vn\!\cdot\!\nabla)\theta
  =\cos\theta\,\partial_x\theta+\sin\theta\,\partial_y\theta,\qquad
  |\zeta(\vx)|\le\zeta_{\rm all},
  \label{eq:curl}
\end{equation}
so $\zeta$ is exactly the signed curvature of the fiber streamlines and
$\zeta_{\rm all}$ is the inverse minimum radius. The spatial derivatives come
\emph{analytically} from the CS-RBF map \eqref{eq:rbf}:
$\partial_x\theta=\mathbf B_x\hat\theta$, $\partial_y\theta=\mathbf B_y\hat\theta$.
Writing $W_{ci}=\phi(q_{ci})$, $S_c=\sum_j W_{cj}$ (so $B_{ci}=W_{ci}/S_c$) and
differentiating the kernel \emph{and} its normalization consistently,
\begin{equation}
  \partial_x W_{ci}=-\frac{20(1-q_{ci})^3}{r^2}\,(x_c-s_{ix}),\qquad
  (\mathbf B_x)_{ci}=\frac{\partial_x W_{ci}}{S_c}
  -B_{ci}\,\frac{\sum_j\partial_x W_{cj}}{S_c},
  \label{eq:rbfderiv}
\end{equation}
and likewise $\mathbf B_y$ (using $\phi'(q)=-20\,q(1-q)^3$); this is
\texttt{csrbf\_operators} in \texttt{wavetopo/dolfinx\_wave.py}. We impose
\eqref{eq:curl} through a
smooth quadratic penalty on the violation,
\begin{equation}
  \Pi(\hat\theta)=\big\langle\,\max\!\big(0,\ \zeta^2-\zeta_{\rm all}^2\big)\,\big\rangle,
  \qquad
  \min_{\hat\theta}\ -\ln E(\hat\theta)+\mu\,\Pi(\hat\theta),
\end{equation}
whose gradient uses the curvature Jacobian
$\partial\zeta/\partial\hat\theta=\operatorname{diag}(-\sin\theta\,\theta_x+\cos
\theta\,\theta_y)\mathbf B+\operatorname{diag}(\cos\theta)\mathbf B_x
+\operatorname{diag}(\sin\theta)\mathbf B_y$ (finite-difference verified to
$\sim\!10^{-10}$), with the penalty weight $\mu$ raised by continuation until the
constraint is met.

\section{Complete algorithmic flow}
\label{sec:algo}
This section collects the entire pipeline --- every operator, every factor in the
gradient, and the order in which they are applied --- so both solvers can be
re-derived and re-implemented from the text alone.
\S\ref{sec:algo:ops} defines the regularization and aggregation operators,
\S\ref{sec:algo:chain} composes their derivatives into the two total gradients,
\S\ref{sec:algo:res} fixes the discretization hierarchy, and
\S\ref{sec:algo:pseudo}--\S\ref{sec:algo:flow} give the pseudocode and the
data-flow diagram.

\subsection{Operator inventory}
\label{sec:algo:ops}
Six linear or smooth nonlinear maps stand between the raw optimizer variables and
the physics. Each contributes one transposed factor to the gradient.

\paragraph{(i) CS-RBF orientation map $\mathbf B$.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:rbf}: $\theta=\mathbf B\hat\theta$, row-stochastic
($\sum_i B_{ci}=1$), with the analytic spatial derivatives
$\mathbf B_x,\mathbf B_y$ of \eqref{eq:rbfderiv}. Adjoint factor: $\mathbf B\T$.

\paragraph{(ii) Mirror-symmetry projector $\mathbf S$.}
Let $\bm\Pi$ be the permutation exchanging support sites reflected about the
symmetry axis. A fiber field is mirror-symmetric iff
$\hat\theta=-\bm\Pi\hat\theta$, so the orthogonal projector onto that subspace is
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf S=\tfrac12\bigl(\mathbf I-\bm\Pi\bigr),\qquad
  \bm\Pi^2=\mathbf I\ \Longrightarrow\
  \mathbf S^2=\mathbf S=\mathbf S\T ,
  \label{eq:sym}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ $\mathbf S$ is idempotent and self-adjoint, so applying it to the design
each iteration and to the gradient are the \emph{same} operation --- symmetry
costs nothing and biases nothing.

\paragraph{(iii) Density filter $\mathbf P$ (periodic).}
With cell centroids $\vx_i$, areas $A_i$, radius $R$, and the \emph{minimum-image}
(toroidal) separation $\bm\delta_{ij}=\min\bigl(|\vx_i-\vx_j|,\ \mathbf L-|\vx_i-
\vx_j|\bigr)$ taken componentwise on a cell of size $\mathbf L=(a,b)$,
\begin{equation}
  \tilde y=\mathbf Pz,\qquad
  P_{ij}=\frac{w_{ij}A_j}{\sum_k w_{ik}A_k},\qquad
  w_{ij}=\max\bigl(0,\ R-\lVert\bm\delta_{ij}\rVert\bigr).
  \label{eq:filter}
\end{equation}
$\mathbf P$ is row-stochastic and, through the minimum image, \emph{wraps around
the unit cell}: the physical density is periodic by construction, which is what
makes the optimized cell tile seamlessly. Adjoint factor: $\mathbf P\T$.

\paragraph{(iv) Passive-frame mask $\mathbf E_{\mathcal F}$.}
For the metamaterial we hold a set $\mathcal F$ of wall elements solid. With
$\mathbf E_{\mathcal F}=\operatorname{diag}(\mathbb 1[e\notin\mathcal F])$,
\begin{equation}
  y=\mathbf E_{\mathcal F}\,\tilde y+\mathbf 1_{\mathcal F},
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial y}{\partial\tilde y}=\mathbf E_{\mathcal F},
  \label{eq:passive}
\end{equation}
so the frame is fixed at $y_e=1$ and its gradient rows are annihilated --- the
optimizer never spends design freedom on the frame, and the tiled medium is
guaranteed connected across all four walls.

\paragraph{(v) Threshold projection (as-built topology).}
To report a crisp two-phase part we push the filtered field through the smoothed
Heaviside
\begin{equation}
  \bar y=\frac{\tanh(\beta\eta)+\tanh\bigl(\beta(y-\eta)\bigr)}
              {\tanh(\beta\eta)+\tanh\bigl(\beta(1-\eta)\bigr)},
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial\bar y}{\partial y}
  =\frac{\beta\operatorname{sech}^2\bigl(\beta(y-\eta)\bigr)}
        {\tanh(\beta\eta)+\tanh\bigl(\beta(1-\eta)\bigr)} .
  \label{eq:proj}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(vi) KS soft extrema.}
Over a $\mathbf k$-set of size $N_k$, with the shift $v_{\max}=\max_k v_k$ used for
numerical stability,
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{\max}_\rho(\mathbf v)=v_{\max}+\frac1\rho
  \ln\!\sum_k e^{\rho(v_k-v_{\max})},
  \qquad
  \omega_k=\frac{e^{\rho(v_k-v_{\max})}}{\sum_j e^{\rho(v_j-v_{\max})}},
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial\widetilde{\max}_\rho}{\partial v_k}=\omega_k,
  \label{eq:ks}
\end{equation}
with $\sum_k\omega_k=1$, and $\widetilde{\min}_\rho(\mathbf v)=
-\widetilde{\max}_\rho(-\mathbf v)$ whose derivative is the weight vector of
$-\mathbf v$. The aggregation error is bounded and controllable,
\begin{equation}
  \max_k v_k\ \le\ \widetilde{\max}_\rho(\mathbf v)\ \le\
  \max_k v_k+\frac{\ln N_k}{\rho},
  \label{eq:ksbound}
\end{equation}
so KS never \emph{under}-estimates a band edge: the reported bandwidth is
conservative, and $\rho$ is chosen so $\ln N_k/\rho$ is small against the gaps we
are opening ($\rho=15$, $N_k\le22$ gives $\le0.21$).

\subsection{Composition of the two total gradients}
\label{sec:algo:chain}
\paragraph{Chain A --- orientation-only wave control (\texttt{dolfinx}).}
\begin{equation}
  \hat\theta\ \xrightarrow{\ \mathbf S\ }\ \hat\theta^{\rm sym}
  \ \xrightarrow{\ \mathbf B\ }\ \theta
  \ \xrightarrow{\ \mathbf D_f(\theta)\ }\ \mathcal A(\vth)
  \ \xrightarrow{\ \text{solve}\ }\ \vU\ \longrightarrow\ J .
\end{equation}
Writing $d_\ell=\rmd J/\rmd\theta_\ell$ for the per-cell adjoint sensitivity
\eqref{eq:sens}, the objective actually descended (log-scaled, curvature-penalized)
has the total gradient
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_{\hat\theta}\Bigl[-\ln E+\mu\,\Pi\Bigr]
  =-\frac{1}{E}\,\mathbf S\T\mathbf B\T\mathbf d
  \;+\;\mu\,\mathbf S\T\Bigl(\frac{\partial\bm\zeta}{\partial\hat\theta}\Bigr)^{\!\mathsf T}
  \mathbf q,
  \qquad
  q_c=\frac{2\,\zeta_c\,A_c}{\sum_j A_j}\,
  \mathbb 1\bigl[\zeta_c^2>\zeta_{\rm all}^2\bigr],
  \label{eq:chainA}
\end{equation}
with $\partial\bm\zeta/\partial\hat\theta$ the curvature Jacobian of
\S\ref{sec:curl}. Every factor is either a sparse matrix transpose or a diagonal
scaling: the gradient costs one adjoint solve, nothing more.

\paragraph{Chain B --- two-field Bloch co-design (Q4/\texttt{scipy}).}
\begin{equation}
  z\ \xrightarrow{\ \mathbf P\ }\ \tilde y
   \ \xrightarrow{\ \mathbf E_{\mathcal F}\ }\ y
   \ \xrightarrow{\ \rm SIMP\ }\ \mathbf K,\mathbf M,
  \qquad
  \hat\theta\ \xrightarrow{\ \mathbf B\ }\ \theta
   \ \xrightarrow{\ \mathbf D_f\ }\ \mathbf K ,
\end{equation}
then \eqref{eq:bloch} gives $\omega_n(\mathbf k)$ and \eqref{eq:flatobj} gives $J$.
Since \eqref{eq:eigsens} differentiates $\lambda_n=\omega_n^2$, the frequency
sensitivity carries the factor
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\omega_n}{\partial p}=\frac{1}{2\omega_n}\,
  \frac{\partial\lambda_n}{\partial p},\qquad p\in\{y_e,\hat\theta_i\}.
\end{equation}
Collecting the coefficient of each aggregate in \eqref{eq:flatobj} --- note the
shared $\widetilde{\max}_k\omega_b$ appears in both the width and the upper gap,
and $\widetilde{\min}_k\omega_b$ in both the width and the lower gap --- gives the
explicit, sign-consistent derivative
\begin{multline}
  \frac{\partial J}{\partial p}
  =(w_{\rm f}+w_{\uparrow})\sum_k\omega^{+}_{b,k}
    \frac{\partial\omega_b(\mathbf k)}{\partial p}
  -(w_{\rm f}+w_{\downarrow})\sum_k\omega^{-}_{b,k}
    \frac{\partial\omega_b(\mathbf k)}{\partial p}\\
  -\,w_{\uparrow}\sum_k\omega^{-}_{b+1,k}
    \frac{\partial\omega_{b+1}(\mathbf k)}{\partial p}
  +\,w_{\downarrow}\sum_k\omega^{+}_{b-1,k}
    \frac{\partial\omega_{b-1}(\mathbf k)}{\partial p},
  \label{eq:chainB}
\end{multline}
where $\omega^{+}_{n,k}$ and $\omega^{-}_{n,k}$ are the KS weights \eqref{eq:ks} of
$\{\omega_n(\mathbf k)\}_k$ and of $\{-\omega_n(\mathbf k)\}_k$. Adding the volume
term and applying the transposes of (iii)--(iv) and (i),
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_z J=\mathbf P\T\mathbf E_{\mathcal F}
  \Bigl[\frac{\partial J}{\partial y}
  +2c_v(\bar z-v^\star)\frac{\mathbf A}{\textstyle\sum_j A_j}\Bigr],
  \qquad
  \nabla_{\hat\theta}J=\mathbf B\T\frac{\partial J}{\partial\theta}.
  \label{eq:chainBfull}
\end{equation}
Only three bands ($b-1,b,b+1$) are ever differentiated, so the cost per iteration
is $N_k$ eigen-solves plus $O(N)$ element energies.

\subsection{Discretization and design-freedom hierarchy}
\label{sec:algo:res}
Three independent length scales control the problem, and it is useful to keep them
distinct:
\begin{equation}
  \underbrace{h=a/n}_{\text{analysis mesh}}\ \ \le\ \
  \underbrace{R}_{\text{filter radius}}\ \ \lesssim\ \
  \underbrace{h_s=a/k_s}_{\text{orientation support}},
  \qquad
  \underbrace{d}_{\text{tow pitch}} .
  \label{eq:scales}
\end{equation}
The mesh $h$ sets what the \emph{physics} can resolve; the support spacing $h_s$
sets how sharply the \emph{fiber field} may turn (the design's angular
bandwidth); the filter radius $R$ sets the minimum topological feature; and the
tow pitch $d$ sets what the \emph{printer} can lay down. The design-space
dimensions are
\begin{equation}
  \dim z=N=n^2,\qquad \dim\hat\theta=M=k_s^2,\qquad
  n_{\rm tow}\approx a/d ,
\end{equation}
so refining $(n,k_s,d)$ jointly buys topological resolution, orientation
flexibility, and manufacturing granularity at once. This is exactly the sweep
reported in \S\ref{sec:meta:sweep}. The ordering in \eqref{eq:scales} matters:
$R\ge h$ keeps the topology mesh-independent, while $h_s\gtrsim R$ keeps the
orientation field smoother than the topology, so tows never have to turn inside a
feature they cannot resolve.

\subsection{Pseudocode}
\label{sec:algo:pseudo}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Orientation design for wave control (\texttt{dolfinx}, Chain A)}
\label{alg:wave}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require mesh, material $\mathbf Q_f$, frequency $\omega$, damping $\eta$, sponge
  $c(\vx)$, drive $\vb$, support sites $\{\mathbf s_i\}$, radius $r$, curvature
  limit $\zeta_{\rm all}$, void set $\mathcal V$
\State \textbf{mesh:} cut each void in $\mathcal V$ from the geometry
  (\texttt{gmsh}/OCC), refining the rim, so $\mathcal T_h$ conforms to it and the
  hole boundary is traction-free \Comment{no soft void, no staircase}
\State build $\mathbf B,\mathbf B_x,\mathbf B_y$ from \eqref{eq:rbf},
  \eqref{eq:rbfderiv}; build $\mathbf S$ from \eqref{eq:sym}
\State $\hat\theta\gets\mathbf 0$ \Comment{straight-fiber baseline}
\State solve $\vU_{\rm ref}$ on a \emph{separate void-free} mesh and transfer it by
  non-matching interpolation (cloak only); record the baseline metric
\For{$\mu\in\{\mu_0,\dots,\mu_{\rm max}\}$} \Comment{penalty continuation}
  \For{$t=1,\dots,T$}
    \State $\hat\theta\gets\mathbf S\hat\theta$;\quad
           $\theta\gets\mathbf B\hat\theta$
           \Comment{symmetrize, then interpolate}
    \State assemble $\mathcal A(\vth)$ as the real $4\times4$ block
           \eqref{eq:block} \Comment{voids already absent from $\mathcal T_h$}
    \State \textbf{forward:} factor $\mathcal A=\mathbf{LU}$, solve
           $\mathcal A\vU=\vb$
    \State evaluate $J$, and $E$ (focus energy) or the far-field mismatch
    \State \textbf{adjoint:} solve $\mathcal A\T\bm\Lambda=-\partial J/\partial\vU$
           \emph{reusing the factors}
    \State $d_\ell\gets\partial R/\partial\theta_\ell$ by UFL differentiation
           \eqref{eq:sens}
    \State $\bm\zeta\gets\cos\theta\,(\mathbf B_x\hat\theta)
           +\sin\theta\,(\mathbf B_y\hat\theta)$;\quad
           $\Pi\gets\langle\max(0,\zeta^2-\zeta_{\rm all}^2)\rangle$
    \State $\mathbf g\gets$ \eqref{eq:chainA}
           \Comment{$\mathbf S\T\mathbf B\T$ plus curvature term}
    \State $\hat\theta\gets\textsc{MMA}\bigl(\hat\theta,\mathbf g;\
           -\tfrac\pi2\le\hat\theta\le\tfrac\pi2\bigr)$
  \EndFor
  \If{$\max_c|\zeta_c|\le\zeta_{\rm all}$} \textbf{break} \Comment{feasible}
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \textbf{delineate toolpaths:} sign-consistent branch of
  $(\cos2\theta,\sin2\theta)$; solve $\nabla\psi=\mathbf g$ by \texttt{lsqr}
  \eqref{eq:phase}; tows $\chi=\tfrac12+\tfrac12\cos(2\pi\psi/d)$, masked off
  $\mathcal V$
\State \Return $\hat\theta$, $\theta$, $\vU$, gain/reduction, $\chi$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Flat-band $+$ band-gap co-design on the periodic cell (Chain B)}
\label{alg:flat}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require cell $a\times a$, mesh $n\times n$, supports $k_s\times k_s$, filter $R$,
  target band $b$, $\mathbf k$-set $\{\mathbf k_j\}_{j=1}^{N_k}$, weights
  $w_{\rm f},w_\uparrow,w_\downarrow$, volume target $v^\star$, frame width
\State build periodic $\mathbf P$ \eqref{eq:filter}, periodic CS-RBF $\mathbf B$,
  frame set $\mathcal F$ and $\mathbf E_{\mathcal F}$ \eqref{eq:passive}
\State $z\gets z_0$ (uniform $+$ central resonator seed, $z_e=1$ on
  $\mathcal F$);\quad $\hat\theta\gets\mathbf 0$
\State $y\gets\mathbf E_{\mathcal F}\mathbf Pz+\mathbf 1_{\mathcal F}$;
  assemble; compute the \emph{baseline} bands on a fine $\Gamma$--X--M--$\Gamma$ path
\For{$t=1,\dots,T$}
  \State $y\gets\mathbf E_{\mathcal F}\mathbf Pz+\mathbf 1_{\mathcal F}$;\quad
         $\theta\gets\mathbf B\hat\theta$
  \State assemble $\mathbf K(y,\theta)$, $\mathbf M(y)$ (SIMP two-phase)
  \For{$j=1,\dots,N_k$} \Comment{independent across $\mathbf k$}
    \State $\mathbf K_r\gets\mathbf T\HH\mathbf K\mathbf T$,
           $\mathbf M_r\gets\mathbf T\HH\mathbf M\mathbf T$ at $\mathbf k_j$
    \State solve \eqref{eq:bloch} for the lowest $b+3$ pairs
           $(\omega_n,\bm\phi_n)$, $\mathbf M_r$-orthonormal
    \State $\partial\omega_n/\partial(y,\hat\theta)$ from \eqref{eq:eigsens}
           for $n\in\{b-1,b,b+1\}$
  \EndFor
  \State KS weights $\omega^\pm$ \eqref{eq:ks};\quad
         width, gap$_\uparrow$, gap$_\downarrow$, $\bar z$
  \State $J\gets$ \eqref{eq:flatobj};\quad
         $\nabla_zJ,\nabla_{\hat\theta}J\gets$
         \eqref{eq:chainB}--\eqref{eq:chainBfull}
  \State track best iterate with $|\bar z-v^\star|<\varepsilon_v$
  \State $(z,\hat\theta)\gets\textsc{MMA}\bigl((z,\hat\theta),\nabla J;\
         0\le z\le1,\ -\tfrac\pi2\le\hat\theta\le\tfrac\pi2\bigr)$
\EndFor
\State \textbf{as-manufactured check:} project $\bar y$ \eqref{eq:proj}; build the
  exactly periodic phase (FFT part $+$ integer tow cycles) and tows $\chi$; mask to
  $\bar y\ge\tfrac12$; \emph{re-assemble and re-solve} \eqref{eq:bloch} on that geometry
\State \Return $z,\hat\theta$, bands (baseline / SIMP / as-manufactured), $\chi$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Cost per iteration, and why the sweep is budget-limited}
\label{sec:algo:cost}
The two pipelines have very different scaling, and it is worth writing down
because it dictates the experimental design of \S\ref{sec:meta:sweep}.

\paragraph{Chain A.} The forward and adjoint systems share the operator
$\mathcal A$, and the adjoint uses its \emph{transpose}. We therefore factor once
per iteration and reuse the factors, so an iteration costs one sparse LU
factorization plus two triangular solves, plus $O(N)$ element work for the
gradient. Sparse 2D nested dissection gives $O(n_{\rm dof}^{3/2})$ --- mild, and
the reason the wave-control designs run to convergence at full resolution.

\paragraph{Chain B.} Here the cost is dominated by the \emph{dense} Hermitian
eigen-solve of Remark~\ref{rem:solvers}. The reduced system has
$n_{\rm red}=2n^2$ degrees of freedom, and a dense $\mathcal O(n_{\rm red}^3)$
factorization at each of $N_k$ wavevectors gives
\begin{equation}
  T_{\rm iter}\ \sim\ N_k\,n_{\rm red}^3\ =\ 8\,N_k\,n^{6},
  \label{eq:cost}
\end{equation}
a \emph{sixth power} of the mesh refinement. Measured single-wavevector gradient
times on this machine are $0.26$\,s, $2.83$\,s and $24.2$\,s at $n=24,36,48$;
\eqref{eq:cost} predicts ratios $(36/24)^6=11.4$ and $(48/24)^6=64$ against
observed $10.9$ and $93$ --- the $n^6$ law is the right model, with the finest
level running somewhat worse still as the working set outgrows cache.

This is why the levels in Table~\ref{tab:sweep} cannot be given equal budgets: at
fixed wall-clock, \eqref{eq:cost} forces $N_k$ and the iteration count \emph{down}
as $n$ goes up. The consequence --- the finest, most flexible parametrization is
also the least converged and the most coarsely sampled in $\mathbf k$ --- biases
the comparison \emph{against} the hypothesis that more design freedom helps, so any
improvement seen at L3 is obtained despite a handicap, not because of an
advantage. A shift--invert Krylov solve targeting only bands $b\pm1$ would remove
the $n^6$ wall, at the cost of the band-index bookkeeping that the dense subset
solve gives for free; we keep the dense solve here because correct band tracking
during optimization matters more than speed.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
Both experiments use the reference orthotropic material and a plane-wave drive
from the left edge, with the quadratic sponge of \S\ref{sec:sponge} on the other
three sides; only the fiber orientation is designed.

\subsection{Curvilinear-fiber wave lens}
On a $4\times3$ plate ($130\times98$ triangulation, $\omega=40$, $\eta=0.02$) we
maximize the focus energy $E=\int_{\rm reg}|\vu|^2$ over a target region at
$(0.78L_x,\,L_y/2)$ (marked by a dashed circle). The straight-fiber baseline is a bright, coherent standing
wave (focus fraction $1.05\%$); the orientation-optimized design forms a
graded-index lens whose fibers converge symmetrically on the target, producing a
train of focal lobes along the axis that concentrates the field with an
$\mathbf{78\times}$ focus-energy gain (focus fraction $7.7\%$), the gain rising
steadily over the MMA iterations (Figure~\ref{fig:lens}, convergence panel). The
design enforces the
mirror symmetry about $y=L_y/2$ (\S\ref{sec:rbf}); the field and toolpaths are
correspondingly symmetric. The material is homogeneous everywhere --- only the
fiber \emph{direction} varies.

\subsection{Curvature-constrained lens (manufacturable toolpaths)}
\label{sec:curlres}
The unconstrained lens attains its gain partly through very tight fiber bends near
the focus, which a print head could not lay down. Re-solving with the curvature
constraint \eqref{eq:curl} bounds the fiber-path curvature to
$\zeta_{\rm all}$ (here $45\%$ of the unconstrained peak): the peak curvature drops
from $|\zeta|_{\max}=\CURLU$ to $\CURLC\approx\zeta_{\rm all}$, the tight turns are
smoothed into gentle, printable arcs, and the focus gain falls from $\GAINU\times$
to $\GAINC\times$ (Figure~\ref{fig:curl}). This is the expected
manufacturability--performance trade-off of the reference work: enforcing a
finite minimum turning radius costs focusing authority but yields toolpaths a
continuous-fiber printer can actually follow. Both designs remain mirror-symmetric.

\subsection{Multi-target beam shaping (two foci and a null)}
\label{sec:multi}
The same machinery handles a genuinely multi-objective goal by a signed region
weight. We ask the plate to \emph{maximize} energy in two separated focus regions
while \emph{minimizing} it in a third between them: setting $w=+1$ on the two
targets and $w=-\lambda$ on the null ($\lambda=1$) and maximizing
$J=\int_\Omega w|\vu|^2$ drives a fiber beam splitter. The straight-fiber baseline
illuminates the three regions almost equally (target/null contrast $\MCONA$); the
optimized orientation splits the wave into the two off-axis foci and holds the
centre as a local dark node, raising the target/null contrast to $\MCONB$ --- a
$\MCIMP\times$ improvement --- while the two foci themselves brighten
$\MTGAIN\times$ (Figure~\ref{fig:multi}). (The whole field amplifies off the very
dim straight-fiber baseline, so the meaningful measure of the null is its
\emph{contrast} against the neighbouring foci, not its absolute level.) Only the
signed objective changed --- the physics, adjoint, parametrization and toolpath
pipeline are identical to the single-focus lens.

\subsection{Elastic cloak of a conforming traction-free void}
\label{sec:cloak}
On an $8\times5$ plate a circular void of radius $R_V=0.55$ scatters the incident
plane wave, casting a downstream shadow and upstream reflections. As in
\S\ref{sec:hole}, the void is \emph{cut from the geometry}: the \texttt{gmsh} mesh
conforms to the circle (refined on the rim) and the rim is traction-free through
the natural boundary condition. Because the holed mesh carries no material inside
the void, the undisturbed reference $\vu_{\rm ref}$ cannot be recovered by setting
the density to one; it is solved on a \emph{separate hole-free mesh} of the same
plate and transferred by non-matching interpolation
(\texttt{examples/dolfinx\_cloak\_conforming\_vec.py}). At $\omega=50$ the plate
carries $\sim\!12$ wavelengths, so the far field to be reconstructed is a rich
standing-wave pattern rather than a few lobes.

\paragraph{The design is confined to a shell.} Orientation is allowed to change
\emph{only} inside a cloaking shell of radius $R_{\rm des}=1.5$ about the void
(drawn red in Figure~\ref{fig:cloak}); everywhere else the fibers remain exactly
the straight baseline. Pinning the support values alone does not achieve this ---
the CS-RBF has radius $0.6$, so a support near the shell edge still rotates fibers
well outside it --- so we additionally mask the per-cell orientation by a smooth
taper vanishing at $R_{\rm des}$ and carry the same mask through the chain rule,
$\nabla_{\hat\theta}J=\mathbf B\T(\mathbf t\odot\partial J/\partial\theta)$ with
$\mathbf t$ the taper. The driver verifies the result directly, reporting
$\max|\theta|=0$ outside the shell. Note also that the plate is
\emph{taller} than the wave-control studies: on a $6\times3$ plate the shell would
run from $y=0.1$ to $2.9$ while the sponge already begins at $y=0.55$, so the
design region would be clipped top and bottom and the fibers would have no room to
wrap the void vertically.

Optimizing that shell alone to match $\vu_{\rm ref}$ over the far field (outside
$R_C=1.7$, i.e.\ strictly \emph{outside} the region it is allowed to design)
reduces the scattering mismatch $J=\int_{\rm obs}|\vu-\vu_{\rm ref}|^2$ by
$\mathbf{36\times}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:cloak}). The far-field standing-wave pattern
is restored --- shadow refilled, reflections suppressed --- with the residual
disturbance confined to the near field of the void, and the toolpaths curve
smoothly around the void and reconverge to straight fibers at the shell boundary.

Two honest qualifications. First, the reduction is \emph{still rising} at the last
of $240$ iterations, so $36\times$ is a lower bound on what this formulation
reaches rather than a converged optimum. Second, this figure is not comparable to
the $28\times$ of the soft-void treatment or to earlier runs of this example on a
smaller plate: confining the design to a shell, enlarging the domain and raising
the frequency all change the problem. The comparison that \emph{is} meaningful is
the discretization one --- posed with a SIMP density void on a structured mesh
instead of a conforming cut, the same problem gives markedly less cloaking,
because a staircased rim scatters on its own account and a large part of what the
soft-void design cancels is an artefact of the mesh rather than the void.

\section{Designing around prescribed through-holes}
\label{sec:hole}
The reference work ties the curvature limit directly to geometry: a
\emph{prescribed circular void} (a passive, zero-density through-hole) of radius
$R$ imposes a local minimum turning radius on the fibers wrapping it, so the
manufacturable curvature limit is $\zeta_{\rm all}=1/R$. We reproduce this rule in
both wave-control settings.

The holes enter the physics \emph{geometrically}: each disk is cut from the domain
with \texttt{gmsh}/OCC and the triangulation conforms to its boundary
(\S\ref{sec:spaces}, \texttt{wavetopo/dolfinx\_mesh.py}), so the rim is exactly
circular and traction-free through the natural boundary condition of the weak
form, with the element size halved on the rim where the diffraction happens. This
matters more than it may appear. The obvious alternative --- representing the hole
as a SIMP soft void ($z=0$, so $z^p\mathbf D_f\to0$) on a structured grid ---
replaces the circle by the set of elements whose centroid lies inside it. The
resulting boundary is a staircase that scatters off its own corners, concentrates
spurious energy on the rim, and defines the radius only to within one element:
precisely the radius $R$ that the curvature rule $\zeta_{\rm all}=1/R$ is tied to.
Conforming to the geometry is the substantive reason these problems are solved in
\texttt{dolfinx} rather than in the structured Q4 code used for the periodic cell
of \S\ref{sec:meta}, where (the geometry being the density field itself) there is
no boundary to conform to and a structured grid is the better tool.

\subsection{Wave lens with two circular through-holes}
\label{sec:lenshole}
We cut two symmetric circular through-holes ($R=0.28$, so $1/R\approx\HRINV$)
into the beam path of the lens and again maximize the focus energy. Without a
curvature limit the optimizer routes the fibers through the gap between the holes
and around their flanks, refocusing the diffracted field with a
$\HGAINU\times$ gain but at the cost of tight bends where the tows crowd past the
voids ($|\zeta|_{\max}=\HCURLU$). Imposing the through-hole limit
$|\zeta|\le1/R$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:curl}) relaxes those bends to the radius of the
holes themselves: the peak curvature falls to $\HCURLC$, just inside the limit
$1/R=\HRINV$, and the gain settles at $\HGAINC\times$
(Figure~\ref{fig:lenshole}). The toolpaths now wrap the prescribed voids with arcs
no tighter than the holes --- a directly printable layout. Note that the
constrained design is \emph{feasible}, not merely penalized: the reported
$|\zeta|_{\max}$ lies below $\zeta_{\rm all}$ rather than near it from above.

\subsection{Asymmetric through-holes: the design re-optimizes per geometry}
\label{sec:lenshole_asym}
The symmetric layout of Figure~\ref{fig:lenshole} could give the misleading
impression that the fiber field is fixed and only the holes move. It is not: the
orientation is re-optimized from scratch for each hole geometry. To make this
explicit we place two \emph{asymmetric} through-holes --- different centres and
radii, breaking the $y=L_y/2$ mirror symmetry --- and drop the symmetry constraint
on the design entirely (a plain, non-symmetric CS-RBF support grid). The optimizer
returns a visibly \emph{asymmetric} fiber field that threads the wave through the
off-centre gap between the voids and re-focuses on the target, a
$\AGAIN\times$ gain with peak curvature $|\zeta|_{\max}=\ACURL$
(Figure~\ref{fig:lenshole_asym}). Here the curvature limit is set by the
\emph{smaller} of the two holes, $\zeta_{\rm all}=1/0.24=\ALIM$, and we report
honestly that the converged design ends marginally \emph{above} it
($\ACURL$ vs.\ $\ALIM$, a $1.7\%$ overshoot): the constraint is imposed by the
smooth quadratic penalty of \S\ref{sec:curl} under continuation, which drives the
curvature down to the neighbourhood of the bound but does not enforce it exactly.
The symmetric case of \S\ref{sec:lenshole} does land strictly inside its bound
($\HCURLC$ vs.\ $\HRINV$); this one does not quite, and a hard constraint (or a
larger terminal $\mu$) would be required to certify feasibility. The toolpaths and the
director map wrap the two holes differently, exactly as the differing local
geometry demands --- the design is genuinely solved anew, not mirrored.

\subsection{Routing energy around a joint: a curved delivery corridor}
\label{sec:guide}
The through-hole studies so far treat the void as an obstacle to be worked around.
A bolt hole or a fastened joint is more than that: it is the place where a panel
\emph{fails}. Fatigue cracks nucleate at the rim, so vibrational energy
concentrating there is precisely what a designer wants to avoid. This suggests a
design problem the reference work does not pose --- route the energy along a
prescribed \emph{curved} path that detours around the joint, delivering it
downstream while keeping the fatigue-critical rim quiet.

We place the joint (radius $R_J=0.40$, cut conformally from the mesh) squarely on
the straight line between the drive and a delivery port, so the direct path is
blocked and the energy \emph{must} detour. Three regions define the goal: a curved
corridor whose centreline is a smooth bump lifting the path over the joint,
\begin{equation}
  y_c(x)=y_J+A\,e^{-((x-x_J)/s)^2},\qquad
  \Omega_{\rm cor}=\bigl\{\,|y-y_c(x)|<h\,\bigr\},
  \label{eq:corridor}
\end{equation}
an exit port $\Omega_{\rm exit}$ downstream of the joint, and a guard annulus
$\Omega_{\rm gd}=\{R_J\le r<R_J+g\}$ hugging the rim. Both objectives are carried
by a \emph{single signed} region weight, exactly the device used for the
multi-target lens,
\begin{equation}
  w(\vx)=\underbrace{\mathbb 1_{\Omega_{\rm cor}\cup\Omega_{\rm exit}}(\vx)}
         _{\text{deliver here}}
  \;-\;\lambda\underbrace{\mathbb 1_{\Omega_{\rm gd}}(\vx)}_{\text{protect here}},
  \qquad
  \max_{\hat\theta}\ J=\int_\Omega w\,|\vu|^2 ,
  \label{eq:guideobj}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda=2.5$. Note $J$ is genuinely \emph{signed} --- at the straight-fiber
baseline the guard term dominates and $J<0$ --- so this problem is descended with
the raw gradient rather than the log-objective of \S\ref{sec:adj}, which would be
undefined. The fiber curvature is limited to the joint's own radius,
$\zeta_{\rm all}=1/R_J=\GZALL$, so the tows may not bend tighter than the hole
they wrap.

The straight-fiber baseline illustrates the problem: the beam launched at the
aperture diffracts, spreads onto the joint, and barely reaches the port --- the
guard annulus carries $\GRATZ\times$ more energy than the exit, and only
$\GCONFZ\%$ of the downstream energy is in the corridor at all. Designing the
orientation alone raises the delivered energy by $\GEXIT\times$, cuts the guard
energy to $\GGUARD$ of baseline, and lifts the downstream confinement from
$\GCONFZ\%$ to $\mathbf{\GCONFO\%}$: the field becomes a continuous ribbon that
tracks the corridor over the joint and terminates at the port
(Figure~\ref{fig:guide}). The design is feasible, $|\zeta|_{\max}=\GZ$ against the
print-head limit $\GZALL$.

\paragraph{Three modelling choices decide whether this works at all.} Each was
arrived at by a failure, and each is worth stating because the failures are silent
--- they return healthy-looking headline numbers for designs that do the wrong
thing.

\emph{(i) The source must be localized.} With a full-width plane-wave drive most
of the field never enters the corridor, so there is nothing to channel and the
result is a diffraction pattern with a bright spot at the port. A narrow aperture
at the channel mouth is what makes the problem a guiding problem.

\emph{(ii) The curvature limit must be the print head's, not the hole's.} Setting
$\zeta_{\rm all}=1/R_J$ --- the reference work's rule for fibers \emph{wrapping} a
prescribed void --- is the wrong constraint for a guiding channel and is strictly
binding: the optimizer sits pinned at the bound with the trust region collapsed
and the objective still negative. The physically relevant limit is the machine's
minimum turning radius, here $\zeta_{\rm all}=1/R_{\rm tow}=\GZALL$; the converged
design uses $\GZ$, i.e.\ nearly three times the curvature the hole rule would have
allowed.

\emph{(iii) The delivery reward must saturate.} With a reward linear in
$E_{\rm exit}/E_{\rm exit}^0$ the delivered energy reaches $6300\times$ baseline,
so that single term contributes $6300$ against a guard penalty of $8$ --- a
$760\!:\!1$ imbalance --- and the optimizer buys delivery by letting the joint get
$20\times$ \emph{busier}, defeating the purpose. A logarithmic reward
($\log 6300=8.7$) has diminishing returns, so protecting the rim remains worth
doing however bright the port already is. The gradient still costs one adjoint
solve: for $J=\sum_k c_k E_k$ the adjoint weight is $\sum_k(\partial J/\partial
E_k)\,w_k$, with $\partial J/\partial E_{\rm exit}=W_E/E_{\rm exit}$ re-evaluated
at the current iterate.

\paragraph{Confinement is bought by the leakage penalty, and it has an optimum.}

strong and the optimizer suppresses the field rather than steering it: delivery
collapses by more than an order of magnitude while confinement also falls, since a
suppressed field is not a guided one. The intermediate value dominates the whole
sweep on every metric simultaneously.

\subsection{Cloak: the void as the through-hole}
\label{sec:cloakhole}
The cloak already contains a circular void of radius $R_V=0.55$; treating it as
the prescribed through-hole sets the manufacturable curvature target at the
inverse hole-radius scale, $\zeta_{\rm all}=\CKZALL\approx 1/R_V$.

\emph{Scope note.} Unlike \S\ref{sec:cloak}, this curvature study was run on the
\emph{soft-void} variant of the cloak (a SIMP density void on a structured mesh,
\texttt{examples/dolfinx\_cloak\_soft.py}), not on the conforming mesh. The
qualitative conclusion below --- that a cloak cannot meet the exact $1/R_V$ bound
without losing its function --- is a statement about the \emph{geometry} of the
problem (a cloak must wrap the void at least as tightly as the void itself) and is
not an artefact of that choice. The absolute reduction factors quoted here are,
however, on the soft-void baseline and are therefore \emph{not} comparable to the
$36\times$ of \S\ref{sec:cloak}; re-running this study conformally would be
expected to raise them, as it did there. Curvature is
meaningful only where there is
material, so we evaluate and penalize $|\zeta|$ over the solid only (the void is
blanked in the maps of Figure~\ref{fig:cloakhole}). The unconstrained cloak
attains a $\CKREDU\times$ far-field scatter reduction with fibers hugging the void
tightly, reaching a solid peak $|\zeta|_{\max}=\CKCURLU$. Penalizing the curvature
toward the through-hole target drives that peak down to $|\zeta|_{\max}=\CKCURLC$
--- the tight bends around the void are visibly relaxed --- while the cloak retains
a comparable $\CKREDC\times$ reduction (Figure~\ref{fig:cloakhole}). Unlike the
lens, the cloak cannot reach $1/R_V$ exactly: enforcing it as a hard bound
collapses the design back to straight fibers (no cloaking at all), because a cloak
must wrap the void at least as tightly as the void itself. This is the honest,
and sharper, manufacturability--performance trade-off of the cloak --- the curl
penalty buys a large curvature reduction at little cloaking cost, but the last
increment down to the exact hole radius is unaffordable.

\section{Periodic metamaterials: co-designed flat bands and band gaps}
\label{sec:meta}
The wave-control problems above steer a wave through a finite plate. The same
orthotropic fiber design also shapes the intrinsic \emph{dispersion} of a periodic
fiber-composite metamaterial. Here we co-design \emph{two} fields on a single
periodic unit cell --- the density $z$ (topology) and the fiber orientation
$\theta$ (toolpath) --- to make a chosen band \emph{flat} (a slow-wave,
high-density-of-states mode) and \emph{isolated} by gaps to its neighbours. The
constitutive model is identical to \S\ref{sec:model} (the rotated orthotropic
$\mathbf D_f(\theta)$ with SIMP density), but the solver is a different one.

\begin{remark}[Two independent solvers]
\label{rem:solvers}
The results of \S\ref{sec:results}--\S\ref{sec:hole} are computed in
\texttt{dolfinx}/\texttt{FEniCSx} on an unstructured triangulation with
\texttt{PETSc} linear algebra and UFL-generated adjoints
(\texttt{wavetopo/dolfinx\_wave.py}). The band-structure results of this section are
\emph{not}: they come from an independent, self-contained
\texttt{numpy}/\texttt{scipy} Q4 quadrilateral code
(\texttt{wavetopo/cfrp.py}$\rightarrow$\texttt{wavetopo/bloch.py}) that imports no
\texttt{dolfinx}, \texttt{UFL}, \texttt{PETSc} or \texttt{SLEPc}. Bloch
periodicity is imposed by an explicitly constructed complex reduction matrix
$\mathbf T(\mathbf k)$, and \eqref{eq:bloch} is solved as a \emph{dense} Hermitian
generalized eigenproblem (\texttt{scipy.linalg.eigh} on a band subset) --- which is
why the per-$\mathbf k$ cost grows steeply with mesh size
(Table~\ref{tab:sweep}). The two codes therefore share the \emph{physics} (the same
$\mathbf Q_f$, the same $\mathbf D_f(\theta)=\mathbf T\mathbf Q_f\mathbf T\T$, the
same SIMP interpolation) and the same design machinery (CS-RBF map, density
filter, MMA), but no assembly or linear-algebra code whatsoever. That independence
is worth stating plainly rather than eliding: each implementation is a check on the
other's constitutive algebra, and neither set of results inherits a bug from the
other.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Bloch--Floquet reduction and the two-field cell}
On a rectangular cell of size $a\times b$ a Bloch wave obeys
$\vu(\vx+\mathbf R)=e^{\ii\mathbf k\cdot\mathbf R}\vu(\vx)$ for lattice vectors
$\mathbf R$. Assembling the full-cell stiffness and mass $\mathbf K(z,\theta)$,
$\mathbf M(z)$ from the two-phase interpolation
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf K_e=\bigl(\epsilon+(1-\epsilon)z_e^{\,p}\bigr)\mathbf k_f(\theta_e)
  +\bigl(1-z_e^{\,p}\bigr)\mathbf k_m,\qquad
  \mathbf M_e=\bigl(\rho_{\min}+z_e(1-\rho_{\min})\bigr)\rho_f\,\mathbf m_e
\end{equation}
(stiff anisotropic fiber phase $\mathbf k_f(\theta)$ vs.\ soft isotropic matrix
$\mathbf k_m$), Bloch periodicity is imposed by a complex reduction matrix
$\mathbf T(\mathbf k)$ that ties each boundary node to its master with the phase
$e^{\ii\mathbf k\cdot\mathbf R}$. The reduced Hermitian generalized eigenproblem is
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf K_r(\mathbf k)\,\bm\phi_n=\omega_n^2(\mathbf k)\,\mathbf M_r(\mathbf k)\,
  \bm\phi_n,\qquad
  \mathbf K_r=\mathbf T\HH\mathbf K\,\mathbf T,\quad
  \mathbf M_r=\mathbf T\HH\mathbf M\,\mathbf T,
  \label{eq:bloch}
\end{equation}
solved for the lowest bands along a Brillouin-zone path $\Gamma$--X--M--$\Gamma$.
The anisotropy of $\mathbf k_f(\theta)$ is what makes the gaps
\emph{direction-dependent}, giving orientation genuine design authority over the
dispersion.

\subsection{Eigenvalue sensitivities and the flat-band objective}
For a simple eigenvalue $\lambda_n=\omega_n^2$ with $\mathbf M_r$-orthonormal
$\bm\phi_n$, first-order perturbation gives the design derivative
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\lambda_n}{\partial p}
  =\bm\phi_n\HH\Bigl(\frac{\partial\mathbf K_r}{\partial p}
  -\lambda_n\frac{\partial\mathbf M_r}{\partial p}\Bigr)\bm\phi_n,
  \qquad p\in\{z_e,\ \hat\theta_i\},
  \label{eq:eigsens}
\end{equation}
which reduces to element energies of $\partial\mathbf k_f/\partial\theta$
\eqref{eq:dDf} and the SIMP derivatives (\texttt{bloch.py : eigen\_sensitivity},
FD-verified). To make band $b$ flat and isolated we aggregate over the $\mathbf k$
set with Kreisselmeier--Steinhauser (KS) soft extrema and minimize
\begin{equation}
  J=w_{\rm f}\underbrace{\bigl(\widetilde{\max}_k\,\omega_b
  -\widetilde{\min}_k\,\omega_b\bigr)}_{\text{bandwidth of }b}
  -w_{\uparrow}\underbrace{\bigl(\widetilde{\min}_k\,\omega_{b+1}
  -\widetilde{\max}_k\,\omega_b\bigr)}_{\text{gap above}}
  -w_{\downarrow}\underbrace{\bigl(\widetilde{\min}_k\,\omega_b
  -\widetilde{\max}_k\,\omega_{b-1}\bigr)}_{\text{gap below}}
  +c_v(\bar z-v^\star)^2,
  \label{eq:flatobj}
\end{equation}
over $z$ (density-filtered) and $\hat\theta$ (CS-RBF), by the same bound-constrained
MMA (\texttt{wavetopo/flatband\_opt.py}). Minimizing the bandwidth flattens $b$
while the two gap terms push its neighbours away; all three share the
$\widetilde{\max}_k\omega_b$ term consistently.

\subsection{Result: an isolated flat band, and an as-manufactured check}
So the tiled medium is physically connected --- with mass on all four cell walls,
not a set of disjoint struts --- we fix a thin passive solid \emph{frame} of fiber
composite on the cell boundary (a locally-resonant frame enclosing a designed
resonator, the classic flat-band morphology; \texttt{flatband\_opt.py}, the
\texttt{passive} mask). Co-optimizing the interior density and the fiber orientation
then drives band $\FBBAND$ from a bandwidth of $\FBWZ$ to $\FBWO$ (a $\FBFLAT\times$
flattening) and opens isolating gaps on \emph{both} sides ($\FBGU$ above, $\FBGD$
below), so the flat band sits alone in a complete gap
(Figure~\ref{fig:flatband}). The optimized cell tiles seamlessly (periodic density
and orientation) and is connected across every boundary, and both the topology and
the fiber toolpaths are non-trivial --- the flat band is a genuine \emph{co-design}
of where the material sits \emph{and} which way its fibers run, neither alone
sufficing.

To check manufacturability we re-simulate the \emph{as-built} cell: the design
density is projected to a crisp two-phase topology (the ``cropped'' material) and
delineated into finite-pitch fiber tows (\S\ref{sec:rbf}, masked to the solid), and
the band structure is recomputed from that geometry and orientation. The flat band
survives the crop --- its bandwidth is $\FBWV$ (still far below the baseline $\FBWZ$)
and \emph{both} isolating gaps remain ($\FBGUV$ above, $\FBGDV$ below;
Figure~\ref{fig:flatband}, top right) --- confirming the slow-wave mode and its
complete gap are properties of the manufacturable part, not of the intermediate
SIMP densities.

\subsection{Resolution and toolpath-pitch sweep: what extra design freedom buys}
\label{sec:meta:sweep}
The design freedom in this problem is set by the orientation-support count
$M=k_s^2$, the filter radius $R$, and the tow pitch $d$ of \eqref{eq:scales}. The
analysis mesh $n$ is \emph{not} a design variable --- it is what the physics is
resolved on --- so we hold it fixed at $n=32$, along with the iteration budget,
and vary only the parametrization. This matters: an earlier version of this
experiment refined $n$ together with $(k_s,R,d)$, which forced $N_k$ and the
iteration count \emph{down} as the design freedom went up (the $n^6$ cost of
\eqref{eq:cost}), confounding the two effects beyond recovery.
Table~\ref{tab:sweep} reports the controlled comparison.

\input{sweep_table.tex}

\paragraph{Design freedom helps --- but only if the $\mathbf k$-sampling keeps up.}
Rows C1--C3 share one $\mathbf k$-set ($N_k=22$), so they isolate the
parametrization. Going from $M=16$ to $M=36$ improves the band as expected
($w_1=0.66\to0.56$). Going on to $M=64$ (row C3) does not: the true width
\emph{degrades to} $0.98$, worse than the coarsest design, and both isolating gaps
nearly vanish. Row C3d is the control --- the \emph{same} $M=64$ parametrization,
same mesh, same iterations, with the $\mathbf k$-set enlarged to $N_k=40$ --- and
it is the best row in the table on every metric ($w_1=0.55$,
$g_\uparrow=+0.53$, $g_\downarrow=+0.67$). The finest parametrization was never
the problem; sampling it with $22$ wavevectors was.

The mechanism is \emph{overfitting of the aggregation set}. The KS extrema in
\eqref{eq:flatobj} constrain the band only at the $N_k$ sampled wavevectors. With
$64$ design variables against $22$ samples the design is over-parametrized, and
the optimizer flattens precisely at the samples while the band excurses between
them --- the exact failure the discretization hierarchy \eqref{eq:scales} is meant
to prevent, appearing in the $\mathbf k$-domain rather than the spatial one.

\begin{remark}[A free overfitting diagnostic]
\label{rem:overfit}
The KS bound \eqref{eq:ksbound} makes this detectable at no extra cost. Because
$\widetilde{\max}_\rho$ can only \emph{over}-estimate a maximum (and
$\widetilde{\min}_\rho$ under-estimate a minimum), the aggregated bandwidth is an
upper bound on the true width \emph{over the sampled set}. If the width measured
on a fine path nevertheless comes out \emph{larger} than the aggregate, the band
must be excursing between samples: the design has overfitted the $\mathbf k$-set.
In Table~\ref{tab:sweep} the aggregate widths at the final iterate are $0.79$,
$0.70$, $0.67$ and $0.68$ for C1, C2, C3, C3d, against fine-path widths of
$0.66$, $0.56$, $\mathbf{0.98}$ and $0.55$. Only C3 has fine $>$ aggregate, and
C3 is exactly the row that fails. The test needs no extra optimization --- only one
fine-path evaluation of the converged design.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Finer tows buy manufacturing fidelity.} The as-manufactured columns
separate the designs again, and along the axis one would expect: the tow pitch.
C1, delineated at the coarse pitch $d=1/6$, does not survive its own toolpath ---
its upper gap goes from $+0.16$ to $\mathbf{-0.14}$, i.e.\ the gap the design was
optimized for \emph{closes} once the fibers are rendered at finite spacing. C2
($d=1/9$) survives with both gaps intact, and C3d ($d=1/12$) is essentially
unchanged by manufacturing ($w_1=0.55\to0.55$, gaps $+0.53/+0.67\to+0.59/+0.57$).
Finer toolpath spacing is therefore not a cosmetic refinement: at $d=1/6$ the
manufactured part does not have the property the design was optimized for.

\section{Verification and reproducibility}
Every sensitivity is derived by the discrete adjoint \eqref{eq:adj} and verified
against central finite differences to a relative error $\sim\!10^{-6}$ on random
cells (\texttt{examples/dolfinx\_lens.py fdcheck}). The complex operator is
assembled exactly as the real block \eqref{eq:block} in a real \texttt{PETSc}
build. The lens and cloak drivers (\texttt{examples/dolfinx\_lens.py},
\texttt{examples/dolfinx\_cloak\_soft.py}) reproduce, in \texttt{dolfinx} and with
a fully automatic UFL adjoint, the two canonical demonstrations of the reference
numpy implementation (\texttt{examples/wave\_lens.py},
\texttt{examples/wave\_cloak.py}): the $\sim\!20\times$ single-frequency lens and
the $\sim\!15\times$ cloak, here at $78\times$ and $36\times$ (the cloak on a conforming rather than soft-void mesh; \S\ref{sec:cloak}).

\section{Conclusion}
A real four-field split lets a real \texttt{PETSc}/\texttt{dolfinx} build solve
the complex in-plane orthotropic time-harmonic problem, and automatic UFL
differentiation of the residual delivers the exact discrete-adjoint orientation
sensitivity \eqref{eq:adj}--\eqref{eq:sens} in two lines, verified to
$\sim\!10^{-6}$. With the genuine vector physics (fiber contrast
$E_{f1}/E_{f2}\approx15$), a light structural damping, and a smooth CS-RBF
orientation parametrization, orientation-only design reproduces the reference
work's canonical results: a symmetric $78\times$ focus-energy wave lens and a $36\times$
elastic cloak, both with smooth manufacturable fiber toolpaths. All of it is a
strict consequence of the fiber orthotropy: by Remark~\ref{rem:iso} the identical
machinery yields \emph{exactly} the baseline for an isotropic plate, so
anisotropy --- supplied physically by the continuous-fiber toolpath --- is the
necessary and sufficient enabling mechanism for orientation-based elastic wave
control.

\section{Detailed derivations (modeling)}
\label{app:derivmodel}
This appendix carries every modeling step in full, so each equation of
\S\ref{sec:model}--\S\ref{sec:sponge} can be re-derived and independently checked.

\subsection{Orthotropic plane-stress stiffness $\mathbf Q_f$}
In the fiber (material) frame $(1,2)$, under plane stress
($\sigma_{33}=\sigma_{13}=\sigma_{23}=0$), the orthotropic compliance relating the
engineering strain $\veps=[\varepsilon_{11},\varepsilon_{22},\gamma_{12}]\T$ to the
stress $\bm\sigma=[\sigma_{11},\sigma_{22},\sigma_{12}]\T$ is
\begin{equation}
  \veps=\mathbf S\,\bm\sigma,\qquad
  \mathbf S=\begin{bmatrix} 1/E_{f1} & -\nu_{21}/E_{f2} & 0\\[1pt]
  -\nu_{12}/E_{f1} & 1/E_{f2} & 0\\[1pt] 0 & 0 & 1/G_{12}\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
with the reciprocity $\nu_{12}/E_{f1}=\nu_{21}/E_{f2}$, i.e.\
$\nu_{21}=\nu_{12}E_{f2}/E_{f1}$, which makes $\mathbf S$ symmetric. The stiffness
is $\mathbf Q_f=\mathbf S^{-1}$. The upper $2\times2$ block inverts with determinant
$\Delta=\tfrac{1}{E_{f1}E_{f2}}(1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21})$, giving
\begin{equation}
  Q_{11}=\frac{E_{f1}}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\quad
  Q_{22}=\frac{E_{f2}}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\quad
  Q_{12}=\frac{\nu_{12}E_{f2}}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\quad Q_{66}=G_{12},
\end{equation}
the entries of \eqref{eq:Df} (\texttt{Qf\_matrix}). The shear row decouples, hence
$Q_{16}=Q_{26}=0$.

\subsection{Rotation to the global frame and $\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial\theta$}
Let $\theta$ be the fiber angle and $(c,s)=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$. Rotating the
material-frame engineering strain to the global frame is $\veps_{\rm mat}=\mathbf
T(\theta)\T\veps$ with the engineering-strain rotation
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf T(\theta)=\begin{bmatrix} c^2 & s^2 & -2sc\\ s^2 & c^2 & 2sc\\
  sc & -sc & c^2-s^2\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
so that the global stiffness is a congruence transform of the material one,
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf D_f(\theta)=\mathbf T(\theta)\,\mathbf Q_f\,\mathbf T(\theta)\T .
  \label{eq:Dfcong}
\end{equation}
Differentiating entry-by-entry ($c'=-s$, $s'=c$),
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf T'(\theta)=\frac{\rmd\mathbf T}{\rmd\theta}
  =\begin{bmatrix} -2sc & 2sc & -2(c^2-s^2)\\ 2sc & -2sc & 2(c^2-s^2)\\
  c^2-s^2 & -(c^2-s^2) & -4sc\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
and by the product rule on \eqref{eq:Dfcong},
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\mathbf D_f}{\partial\theta}
  =\mathbf T'\mathbf Q_f\mathbf T\T+\mathbf T\mathbf Q_f\mathbf T'^{\,\T},
  \label{eq:dDf}
\end{equation}
which is the object every orientation sensitivity is built from
(cf.\ \eqref{eq:sens}).

\subsection{Isotropy annihilation (proof of Remark~\ref{rem:iso})}
$\mathbf Q_f$ is isotropic iff $Q_{11}=Q_{22}$ and $Q_{66}=\tfrac12(Q_{11}-Q_{12})$.
Then $\mathbf Q_f=\lambda\mathbf 1\mathbf 1\T+2\mu\,\mathbf P$ with $\mathbf
1=[1,1,0]\T$ and $\mathbf P$ the deviatoric projector, both invariant under the
in-plane rotation $\mathbf T(\theta)$ (an isotropic tensor is unchanged by
rotation): $\mathbf T\mathbf Q_f\mathbf T\T=\mathbf Q_f$ for all $\theta$. Hence
$\mathbf D_f(\theta)\equiv\mathbf Q_f$ and, by \eqref{eq:dDf},
$\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial\theta\equiv\mathbf 0$; by \eqref{eq:sens} every
orientation sensitivity vanishes. Orientation is a dead variable exactly when the
material is isotropic. $\square$

\subsection{Elastodynamics, time-harmonic reduction, damping, sponge}
Momentum balance for the displacement $\vu(\vx,t)$ with mass density $\rho$ and
the plane-stress constitutive law $\bm\sigma=\mathbf D_f(\theta)\veps(\vu)$ is
$\nabla\!\cdot\!\bm\sigma=\rho\,\ddot{\vu}$. Seeking time-harmonic motion
$\vu(\vx,t)=\Real\{\vu(\vx)e^{-\ii\omega t}\}$ turns $\ddot{\vu}\to-\omega^2\vu$.
Structural (hysteretic) damping multiplies the stiffness by $(1+\ii\eta)$, and a
mass-proportional absorbing sponge $c(\vx)\ge0$ adds a term $-\ii\omega c\,\vu$
(the $\dot{\vu}\to-\ii\omega\vu$ of a viscous force $c\dot\vu$), giving the strong
form \eqref{eq:strong}. Multiplying by a test function $\vv$ and integrating by
parts (the sponge edges carry the outgoing radiation, the driven edge the source)
yields the weak form and, after $P_1$ interpolation, the complex system
\eqref{eq:complex} with
$\mathbf K=\int\veps(\cdot)\T\mathbf D_f\veps(\cdot)$,
$\mathbf M=\int\rho(\cdot)\!\cdot\!(\cdot)$,
$\mathbf C=\int c(\cdot)\!\cdot\!(\cdot)$ (\texttt{\_forms}). The SIMP
interpolation $\mathbf D_f\!\mapsto\!(z_\varepsilon+(1-z_\varepsilon)\hat z^p)
\mathbf D_f$, $\rho\!\mapsto\!\hat z\rho_f+(1-\hat z)\rho_m$ (\texttt{\_zp},
\texttt{\_rho\_eff}) carves the soft void.

\subsection{Exact real four-field split}
Write the complex operator of \eqref{eq:complex} as
$\mathbf D=(1+\ii\eta)\mathbf K-\omega^2\mathbf M+\ii\omega\mathbf C=A+\ii B$ with
\begin{equation}
  A=\mathbf K-\omega^2\mathbf M,\qquad B=\eta\mathbf K+\omega\mathbf C,
\end{equation}
both real symmetric. Splitting $\vu=\vu_r+\ii\vu_i$ and $\mathbf f=\mathbf
f_r+\ii\mathbf f_i$, the equation $(A+\ii B)(\vu_r+\ii\vu_i)=\mathbf f_r+\ii\mathbf
f_i$ separates into real and imaginary parts,
\begin{equation}
  A\vu_r-B\vu_i=\mathbf f_r,\qquad B\vu_r+A\vu_i=\mathbf f_i,
\end{equation}
i.e.\ the real block system \eqref{eq:block} on
$\vU=(u_{xr},u_{yr},u_{xi},u_{yi})$. It is exactly $[\Real,-\Imag;\Imag,\Real]$ of
$\mathbf D$, so a real \texttt{PETSc} build solves the complex problem without
complex scalars.

\section{Detailed derivations (optimization)}
\label{app:derivopt}

\subsection{Discrete adjoint and design sensitivity}
Let the assembled forward problem be $\mathcal A(\vth)\vU=\vb$ with
$\mathcal A=[A,-B;B,A]$, and let $J(\vU)$ be an objective. Form the Lagrangian with
a multiplier $\bm\Lambda$,
$\mathcal L=J(\vU)+\bm\Lambda\T(\mathcal A\vU-\vb)$; on the constraint manifold
$\mathcal L=J$. Its total derivative in a design $\theta_\ell$ is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\rmd J}{\rmd\theta_\ell}
  =\frac{\partial J}{\partial\vU}\T\frac{\rmd\vU}{\rmd\theta_\ell}
  +\bm\Lambda\T\Bigl(\mathcal A\frac{\rmd\vU}{\rmd\theta_\ell}
  +\frac{\partial\mathcal A}{\partial\theta_\ell}\vU\Bigr)
  =\Bigl(\frac{\partial J}{\partial\vU}+\mathcal A\T\bm\Lambda\Bigr)\T
  \frac{\rmd\vU}{\rmd\theta_\ell}
  +\bm\Lambda\T\frac{\partial\mathcal A}{\partial\theta_\ell}\vU .
\end{equation}
Choosing $\bm\Lambda$ to annihilate the bracket gives the adjoint equation and
reduces the sensitivity to the explicit design dependence of the residual
$R(\vth)=\vU\T\!\mathcal A(\vth)\vU-\vb\T\vU$ (with $\vU,\bm\Lambda$ frozen):
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal A\T\bm\Lambda=-\frac{\partial J}{\partial\vU},\qquad
  \frac{\rmd J}{\rmd\theta_\ell}=\bm\Lambda\T
  \frac{\partial\mathcal A}{\partial\theta_\ell}\vU .
\end{equation}
Only $\mathbf K(\vth)$ (hence $A,B$) depends on $\theta$, and only through
$\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial\theta$ \eqref{eq:dDf}; expanding $\bm\Lambda=(\bm
\lambda_r,\bm\lambda_i)$ block-wise and using $\partial A/\partial\theta_\ell=
\partial\mathbf K/\partial\theta_\ell$, $\partial B/\partial\theta_\ell=\eta\,
\partial\mathbf K/\partial\theta_\ell$ gives the explicit cell integral
\eqref{eq:sens}. The mass, sponge and source terms carry no $\theta$ and drop out,
so the gradient is entirely built from $\partial\mathbf D_f/\partial\theta$ ---
which vanishes for an isotropic material, making Remark~\ref{rem:iso} a statement
about the gradient itself. For a quadratic objective $J=\int w|\vu|^2$ the adjoint
load is linear, $\partial J/\partial\vU=2\,\mathrm{diag}(w)\vU$ (a signed weight
$w$ giving the multi-target objective of \S\ref{sec:multi}); in the code both the
adjoint load and the residual derivative are single \texttt{ufl.derivative} calls
(\texttt{\_sensitivity}), FD-verified to $\sim\!10^{-6}$.

\subsection{CS-RBF chain rule and its analytic derivatives}
With $\theta=B\hat\theta$ \eqref{eq:rbf}, the reduced gradient is
$\partial J/\partial\hat\theta=B\T(\partial J/\partial\theta)$. For the curvature
constraint we also need $\partial_x\theta,\partial_y\theta$. Writing
$W_{ci}=\phi(q_{ci})$ and $S_c=\sum_jW_{cj}$ so $B_{ci}=W_{ci}/S_c$, the chain rule
on $q_{ci}=\lVert\vx_c-\mathbf s_i\rVert/r$ with $\phi'(q)=-20q(1-q)^3$ gives
$\partial_x W_{ci}=\phi'(q_{ci})\,\partial_x q_{ci}
=-\tfrac{20(1-q_{ci})^3}{r^2}(x_c-s_{ix})$, and the quotient rule on $B_{ci}$
yields \eqref{eq:rbfderiv}, i.e.\ $\mathbf B_x=S^{-1}W_x-\mathrm{diag}(S^{-1}W_x
\mathbf 1)\,B$ (and $\mathbf B_y$ likewise); the second term is the derivative of
the partition-of-unity normalization, without which $\sum_i(\mathbf B_x)_{ci}\neq0$
and the curvature would be biased (\texttt{csrbf\_operators}).

\subsection{Fiber-curvature functional and its Jacobian}
The fiber-path curvature is the directional derivative of $\theta$ along the fiber
$\vn=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$,
$\zeta=\cos\theta\,\theta_x+\sin\theta\,\theta_y$ with $\theta_x=\mathbf
B_x\hat\theta$, $\theta_y=\mathbf B_y\hat\theta$. Differentiating w.r.t.\
$\hat\theta$ (product rule, $\partial\theta/\partial\hat\theta=B$),
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\zeta}{\partial\hat\theta}
  =\mathrm{diag}(-\sin\theta\,\theta_x+\cos\theta\,\theta_y)\,B
  +\mathrm{diag}(\cos\theta)\,\mathbf B_x+\mathrm{diag}(\sin\theta)\,\mathbf B_y .
\end{equation}
The penalty $\Pi=\langle\max(0,\zeta^2-\zeta_{\rm all}^2)\rangle$ has gradient
$\partial\Pi/\partial\hat\theta=\tfrac{2}{n}(\partial\zeta/\partial\hat\theta)\T
[\zeta\odot\mathbf 1_{\{\zeta^2>\zeta_{\rm all}^2\}}]$ (restricted to solid cells
for the cloak), FD-verified to $\sim\!10^{-10}$ (\texttt{curl\_penalty}). It is
added to the objective with a continuation weight $\mu$ ramped until the
constraint is met.

\subsection{Phase-field toolpath extraction}
The tow field is the level set of $\psi$ with $\nabla\psi=\mathbf g=[-\sin\theta,
\cos\theta]\T$ (unit, orthogonal to the fiber). This overdetermined system
(two equations, one unknown) is solved in the least-squares sense
$\min_\psi\int\lVert\nabla\psi-\mathbf g\rVert^2$. Its Euler--Lagrange condition is
the Poisson equation $\nabla^2\psi=\nabla\!\cdot\mathbf g$ with the \emph{natural}
Neumann data $\partial\psi/\partial n=\mathbf g\!\cdot\!\vn$ on $\partial\Omega$.
The boundary flux is essential: dropping it (homogeneous Neumann) leaves only
$\nabla^2\psi=\nabla\!\cdot\mathbf g$, whose solution for a nearly divergence-free
$\mathbf g$ collapses to $\psi\approx\mathrm{const}$ ($|\nabla\psi|\!\to\!0$), i.e.\
washed-out tows. We therefore assemble the discrete forward-difference gradient
operator $G=[G_x;G_y]$ on the grid and solve $\min_\psi\lVert G\psi-\mathbf
g\rVert^2$ directly (\texttt{lsqr}, one pinned node fixing the constant), which
enforces $|\nabla\psi|\approx1$ and hence a constant tow pitch. The tows are the
peaks of $\chi=\tfrac12+\tfrac12\cos(2\pi\psi/d)$ (\texttt{fiber\_phase}). A
seam-free director branch is obtained beforehand by region-growing sign
propagation (\texttt{\_orient\_consistent}).

\subsection{Exactly periodic phase on a unit cell}
\label{app:perphase}
On the metamaterial cell the construction above must be modified: no node may be
pinned, because $\psi$ is not periodic --- it must advance by a whole number of tow
cycles per lattice vector, or the tows will not join across the boundary. Split
\begin{equation}
  \Psi(\vx)=\psi_p(\vx)+n_x\frac{x}{a}+n_y\frac{y}{b},
  \qquad \psi_p\ \text{cell-periodic},\quad (n_x,n_y)\in\mathbb Z^2 .
  \label{eq:psisplit}
\end{equation}
The periodic part solves $\min_{\psi_p}\int_\Omega\lVert\nabla\psi_p-\mathbf
g/d\rVert^2$, which is \emph{diagonal} in the Fourier basis: with
$\psi_p=\sum_{\mathbf k}\hat\psi_{\mathbf k}e^{\ii\mathbf k\cdot\vx}$ and
$\nabla\mapsto\ii\mathbf k$, the normal equations
$\ii\mathbf k\cdot\overline{(\ii\mathbf k\hat\psi_{\mathbf k})}=
\ii\mathbf k\cdot\hat{\mathbf g}_{\mathbf k}/d$ give
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\ \hat\psi_{\mathbf k}
  =-\ii\,\frac{\mathbf k\cdot\hat{\mathbf g}_{\mathbf k}}{d\,\lVert\mathbf k\rVert^2}
  \ \ (\mathbf k\neq\mathbf 0),\qquad \hat\psi_{\mathbf 0}=0\ }
  \label{eq:fftphase}
\end{equation}
--- the inverse divergence (Helmholtz projection) of $\mathbf g/d$, one FFT pair,
no linear solve. The $\mathbf k=\mathbf 0$ mode is precisely the part no periodic
function can carry, so the \emph{mean} gradient must be absorbed by the linear
term; matching it in the least-squares sense and rounding to the nearest
representable integer,
\begin{equation}
  n_x=\operatorname{round}\!\Bigl(\frac{a\,\langle g_x\rangle}{d}\Bigr),\qquad
  n_y=\operatorname{round}\!\Bigl(\frac{b\,\langle g_y\rangle}{d}\Bigr).
  \label{eq:towcount}
\end{equation}
The rounding is what buys exact periodicity: under $x\mapsto x+a$ the phase
advances by $n_x\in\mathbb Z$, so $\chi=\tfrac12+\tfrac12\cos(2\pi\Psi)$ is
\emph{unchanged}, and the tows join continuously across every cell boundary.

The price is a slight, uniform stretch of the pitch. Since rounding perturbs the
cycle count by at most $\tfrac12$ out of $n_{\rm tow}\approx a/d$ cycles, the
relative pitch error is bounded by
\begin{equation}
  \frac{|\Delta d|}{d}\ \le\ \frac{1}{2\,n_{\rm tow}}\ \approx\ \frac{d}{2a}.
  \label{eq:pitcherr}
\end{equation}
This is a second, independent reason to prefer a fine tow pitch in
\S\ref{sec:meta:sweep}: the coarsest level ($d=1/6$) can suffer up to $8.3\%$
pitch distortion in enforcing periodicity, the finest ($d=1/12$) at most $4.2\%$.
Exact periodicity and faithful pitch are competing goals, reconciled only by
taking $d\ll a$.

\subsection{Properties of the aggregation and symmetry operators}
\label{app:opprops}
\paragraph{KS bounds \eqref{eq:ksbound}.} Since every term of
$\sum_k e^{\rho v_k}$ is positive and one of them is $e^{\rho\max_k v_k}$,
$\sum_k e^{\rho v_k}\ge e^{\rho\max_k v_k}$, so
$\rho^{-1}\ln\sum_k e^{\rho v_k}\ge\max_k v_k$. Conversely every term is at most
$e^{\rho\max_k v_k}$, so $\sum_k e^{\rho v_k}\le N_ke^{\rho\max_k v_k}$ and
$\rho^{-1}\ln\sum_k e^{\rho v_k}\le\max_k v_k+\rho^{-1}\ln N_k$. Hence KS
\emph{over}-estimates a maximum and \emph{under}-estimates a minimum: the
aggregated bandwidth in \eqref{eq:flatobj} is an upper bound on the true sampled
bandwidth, and the aggregated gaps are lower bounds. Both errors are conservative
--- the optimizer is never rewarded for an artefact of the smoothing --- and both
vanish as $\rho^{-1}\ln N_k$. \qed

\paragraph{Symmetry projector \eqref{eq:sym}.} $\bm\Pi$ is an involutive
permutation ($\bm\Pi^2=\mathbf I$, $\bm\Pi\T=\bm\Pi$), so with
$\mathbf S=\tfrac12(\mathbf I-\bm\Pi)$,
$\mathbf S\T=\mathbf S$ and
$\mathbf S^2=\tfrac14(\mathbf I-2\bm\Pi+\bm\Pi^2)=\tfrac12(\mathbf I-\bm\Pi)
=\mathbf S$. Thus $\mathbf S$ is the \emph{orthogonal} projector onto
$\{\hat\theta:\bm\Pi\hat\theta=-\hat\theta\}$, the mirror-symmetric fiber fields.
Being self-adjoint, the same operator symmetrizes the design and the gradient, so
imposing symmetry introduces no inconsistency between the two --- the projected
gradient is exactly the gradient of the restricted problem. \qed

\subsection{Eigenvalue sensitivity for the Bloch metamaterial}
For the generalized Hermitian eigenproblem $\mathbf K_r\bm\phi_n=\lambda_n\mathbf
M_r\bm\phi_n$ (\eqref{eq:bloch}, $\lambda_n=\omega_n^2$), normalize
$\bm\phi_n\HH\mathbf M_r\bm\phi_n=1$. Differentiating the eigen-relation w.r.t.\ a
design $p$ and left-multiplying by $\bm\phi_n\HH$,
\begin{equation}
  \bm\phi_n\HH\mathbf K_r'\bm\phi_n+\bm\phi_n\HH\mathbf K_r\bm\phi_n'
  =\lambda_n'\,\bm\phi_n\HH\mathbf M_r\bm\phi_n
  +\lambda_n\bigl(\bm\phi_n\HH\mathbf M_r'\bm\phi_n
  +\bm\phi_n\HH\mathbf M_r\bm\phi_n'\bigr).
\end{equation}
Because $\bm\phi_n\HH\mathbf K_r=\lambda_n\bm\phi_n\HH\mathbf M_r$ (Hermitian), the
two $\bm\phi_n'$ terms cancel, leaving the Hellmann--Feynman form
$\lambda_n'=\bm\phi_n\HH(\mathbf K_r'-\lambda_n\mathbf M_r')\bm\phi_n$
(\eqref{eq:eigsens}); no eigenvector derivative is needed. With
$\mathbf K_r=\mathbf T\HH\mathbf K\mathbf T$ and the full-cell $\mathbf K,\mathbf M$
element-assembled, $\mathbf K_r',\mathbf M_r'$ reduce to element energies of the
Bloch state $\bm\psi_n=\mathbf T\bm\phi_n$: for orientation
$\partial\mathbf K/\partial\hat\theta_i$ carries $\partial\mathbf k_f/\partial
\theta$ \eqref{eq:dDf} through the CS-RBF map, for density the SIMP derivatives
$p\,z^{p-1}(\mathbf k_f-\mathbf k_m)$ and $(\rho_f-\rho_m)\mathbf m_e$. The band
gradients are then KS-aggregated into \eqref{eq:flatobj}
(\texttt{flatband\_opt.py}).

\subsection{MMA subproblem}
Each iteration the Method of Moving Asymptotes replaces $J,\Pi$ by convex
separable approximations built from moving asymptotes $L_\ell<\hat\theta_\ell<U_\ell$,
\begin{equation}
  \tilde J(\hat\theta)=r+\sum_\ell\Bigl(\frac{p_\ell}{U_\ell-\hat\theta_\ell}
  +\frac{q_\ell}{\hat\theta_\ell-L_\ell}\Bigr),\quad
  \begin{cases} p_\ell=(U_\ell-\hat\theta_\ell^{(k)})^2\,(\partial_\ell J)^+\\
  q_\ell=(\hat\theta_\ell^{(k)}-L_\ell)^2\,(\partial_\ell J)^-\end{cases}
\end{equation}
($(\cdot)^\pm$ the positive/negative parts), with the asymptotes contracted or
expanded by the sign history of $\hat\theta_\ell$ and a move limit per step. The
separable convex subproblem is solved by its (cheap) dual. Because the raw
objectives are $O(10^{-4})$ we feed MMA the scale-invariant log-gradient
$g_\ell=(\partial_{\theta_\ell}J)/J$ for the lens focus, restoring $O(1)$
sensitivities; the cloak uses the raw gradient (well scaled). Bounds are
$\hat\theta_\ell\in[-\tfrac\pi2,\tfrac\pi2]$ (\texttt{cfrp\_optimizer.py : MMA}).
